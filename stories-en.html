October 7 Survivors
Rachel Edri
A 65-year-old grandmother from Ofakim who served tea and cookies to Hamas terrorists for 16 hours — until police arrived.
On October 7, 2023, when Hamas terrorists broke into Rachel Edri's home in the city of Ofakim, she displayed extraordinary composure. Instead of panicking, Rachel — a 65-year-old grandmother — served the terrorists tea and cookies. For 16 long hours, she kept them calm in her living room, chatting with them and preventing them from going out to harm others. Throughout this time, her husband David was tied up in another room. Rachel managed to delay the terrorists until police forces arrived and eliminated them. Her story became a symbol of civilian courage and resourcefulness amid the horror.
BBC News, Oct 2023 | Channel 12 Israel | Times of Israel

Rami Davidian
A farmer from Patish who drove his pickup truck into the Nova massacre and rescued ~200 people under fire.
Rami Davidian, a farmer from Moshav Patish, heard the gunfire on October 7 and immediately drove his pickup truck toward the Nova festival. Over many hours, Rami went back and forth to the massacre site, loading young people onto his truck and driving them to safety — all under live fire from terrorists. By estimates, he rescued approximately 200 people. Rami worked all night and the next day without stopping, even when it was clear his life was in immediate danger. His story became one of the central tales of heroism from that dark day.
CNN, Dec 2023 | NBC News | The Guardian

October 7 Survivors
Nova Festival Survivors
3,500 attendees, 364 murdered, hundreds fled on foot — testimony of Natalie Sanandaji who ran for 5 hours.
The Nova peace festival ("Supernova") took place near Re'im on October 7, 2023, with approximately 3,500 attendees. When rockets began and terrorists arrived, 364 people were murdered and dozens were kidnapped. Hundreds fled on foot through fields and agricultural land. Natalie Sanandaji, one of the survivors, ran on foot for 5 consecutive hours until she reached safety. Her testimony and those of other survivors were documented by the USC Shoah Foundation as part of the largest documentation project since Holocaust survivor testimonies.
USC Shoah Foundation | Reuters, Oct 2023 | The New York Times

Holocaust Survivors
Dr. Edith Eva Eger
Auschwitz survivor at 16 who danced for Mengele, survived, and became a leading psychologist.
Edith Eva Eger was born in Hungary in 1927. At age 16, she was sent to Auschwitz with her family — her parents were murdered on arrival. Mengele himself ordered her to dance for him, and she danced to survive. After a death march and unimaginable suffering, she was liberated in 1945, close to death. Edith emigrated to the US, completed a degree in psychology, and became a leading trauma specialist. Her book "The Choice" (2017) became an international bestseller, describing her journey from Auschwitz to forgiveness, and the choice every person can make — to live as a victim or a survivor.
Edith Eger, "The Choice" (2017) | The New York Times | USC Shoah Foundation

Holocaust Survivors
Elie Wiesel
Nobel Peace Prize laureate, Auschwitz and Buchenwald survivor, who dedicated his life to testimony and memory.
Elie Wiesel (1928–2016) was a writer, professor, and Nobel Peace Prize laureate (1986). At age 15, he and his family were deported to Auschwitz, where his mother and younger sister were murdered. He and his father were transferred to Buchenwald, where his father died shortly before liberation. His book "Night" (1960) became a seminal work of Holocaust literature — a short, devastating account of his experiences in the camps. Wiesel dedicated his remaining life to fighting indifference: "The opposite of love is not hate, it's indifference. The opposite of faith is not heresy, it's indifference." He founded the Foundation for Humanity, lectured worldwide, and refused to be silent.
Elie Wiesel, "Night" (1960) | Nobel Prize Committee, 1986 | USHMM

Lily Ebert
Auschwitz survivor who became a TikTok educator at age 98 — with millions of followers.
Lily Ebert (1923–2024) was sent to Auschwitz in 1944, where Mengele sent her mother and younger sisters directly to the gas chambers. Lily survived forced labor and a death march, and after the war rebuilt her life. At age 97, her grandson persuaded her to start a TikTok account. Lily became a phenomenon — with over 2 million followers. In short, moving videos, she shared her experiences directly with the younger generation. Her book "Lily's Promise" (2021), co-written with her grandson, describes the promise she made — to tell the story so the world would never forget.
Lily Ebert & Dov Forman, "Lily's Promise" (2021) | TikTok @lilyebert | BBC

Holocaust Survivors
Dina Gottliebova Babbitt
An artist forced by Mengele to paint prisoner portraits — who survived through her art.
Dina Gottliebova (1923–2009) was a Czech-Jewish artist sent to Auschwitz in 1943. When Mengele discovered her talent, he ordered her to paint portraits of Roma prisoners — "to document the races" before they were murdered. Dina painted under coercion, but her paintings saved her life and her mother's. After the war, Dina emigrated to the US and became an animator at Warner Bros. studios. In 1973, she discovered that the paintings she had made at Auschwitz were on display at the Auschwitz Museum and demanded their return — a battle that lasted until the end of her life. The paintings remain in the museum.
USHMM | Yad Vashem | The Washington Post | "The Last Album" (2005)

Immigration Stories
Operation Solomon (1991)
14,325 Ethiopian Jews airlifted in 36 hours — a Boeing 747 carried 1,122 passengers (world record).
Operation Solomon took place on May 24–25, 1991, as Ethiopia's government was on the brink of collapse. In just 36 hours, the Israeli Air Force flew 14,325 Ethiopian Jews (Beta Israel) from Addis Ababa to Israel in 34 flights. An El Al Boeing 747 carried 1,122 passengers on a single flight — a world record that stands to this day. Seats were removed to fit more people, and two babies were born during the flight. Operation Solomon is one of the most moving moments in Israeli history — a country sending planes to rescue Jews from the edge of Africa.
Israel State Archives | Jewish Agency | Guinness World Records | IAF Archives

Operation Magic Carpet (1949)
49,000 Yemenite Jews — many had never seen a plane and believed the prophecy "on eagles' wings."
Between June 1949 and September 1950, approximately 49,000 Yemenite Jews were airlifted to Israel in a covert operation called "Magic Carpet" (On Wings of Eagles). Many Yemenite Jews had never seen an airplane — and believed the prophecy "I carried you on eagles' wings" (Exodus 19:4) was literally coming true before their eyes. They left all their belongings, walked hundreds of kilometers to a transit camp in Aden, and were flown to Israel. This was the immigration of an entire community — with a 2,500-year tradition — uprooted from the diaspora and returned home.
Israel State Archives | Jewish Agency | Yemenite Heritage Center | AJHS

Natan Sharansky
Soviet prisoner of Zion whose 9 years in prison couldn't break him — symbol of the Refusenik movement.
Natan (Anatoly) Sharansky, a Soviet Jewish mathematician, was arrested in 1977 on fabricated espionage charges when his only "crime" was wanting to emigrate to Israel. He spent 9 years in Soviet prison, including periods of complete isolation, endless interrogations, and inhumane conditions. His wife Avital led an international campaign for his release that became one of the symbols of the human rights struggle. Sharansky was released in 1986 and immigrated to Israel, where he served as a government minister and chairman of the Jewish Agency. His book "Fear No Evil" (1988) describes how he maintained his inner freedom even while his body was imprisoned.
Natan Sharansky, "Fear No Evil" (1988) | The Guardian | Yad Vashem | Knesset Archives

French Aliyah (2015)
After Charlie Hebdo and Hyper Cacher attacks — 7,900 French Jews made Aliyah, a historic record.
In January 2015, two terror attacks shook France: the massacre at the Charlie Hebdo office (12 killed) and the hostage-taking at the Hyper Cacher supermarket in Paris, where 4 Jews were murdered simply because they were Jewish. The attacks led French Jewry — the third largest Jewish community in the world (~500,000) — to realize that antisemitism had returned to the streets of Europe. In 2015, 7,900 French Jews made Aliyah to Israel, a historic record. Many described a feeling of "living with packed suitcases" — a sensation they hadn't known since the Vichy era.
Jewish Agency, 2015 Report | Le Monde | Times of Israel | CRIF

Tree of Life Synagogue Massacre (2018)
11 worshippers murdered in Pittsburgh — the deadliest antisemitic attack in US history.
On October 27, 2018, a gunman entered the Tree of Life synagogue in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, during Saturday morning services and opened fire on worshippers. 11 people were killed and 6 wounded, including 4 police officers. The shooter shouted "All Jews must die" during the attack. The victims ranged in age from 54 to 97, including a married couple and two brothers. It was the deadliest antisemitic attack in US history. The gunman was convicted of federal hate crimes and sentenced to death. The attack proved that violent antisemitism is not a matter of history books — it is here and now.
ADL, 2018 | FBI Federal Indictment | The New York Times | Pittsburgh Post-Gazette

Campus Antisemitism (2023–24)
ADL documented 8,873 antisemitic incidents (+140%) — Columbia, MIT, Cornell and more.
After October 7, 2023, an unprecedented wave of antisemitism flooded American campuses. The ADL documented 8,873 antisemitic incidents in the US in 2023 — a 140% increase from the previous year. Jewish students at leading universities like Columbia, MIT, Cornell, and Harvard reported harassment, threats, and violence. At Columbia, pro-Palestinian protest camps chanted "Globalize the Intifada" at Jewish students. University presidents were questioned in Congress about their failure to protect Jewish students, and three resigned.
ADL Audit 2023 | Congressional Hearing, Dec 2023 | The Washington Post | AMCHA Initiative

Ilan Halimi (2006)
A 23-year-old French Jew kidnapped and tortured for 24 days in suburban Paris — because "Jews have money."
Ilan Halimi, a 23-year-old French Jew, was kidnapped on January 21, 2006, by the "Gang of Barbarians" led by Youssouf Fofana. The kidnappers chose him specifically because he was Jewish — assuming that "Jews have money and the community will pay ransom." Ilan was held in a Paris suburb for 24 days, during which he was horrifically tortured. Approximately 20 people knew about the kidnapping and did not report it to police. Ilan was found near death by a railway track on February 13 and died en route to the hospital. His mother, Ruth Halimi, wrote "24 Days" and became an activist against antisemitism in France.
Ruth Halimi, "24 Days" (2009) | Le Monde | BBC | CRIF

Jewish Teachers Under Threat in France
Jewish teachers in France live in hiding after death threats — simply because they are Jewish.
In France, Jewish teachers routinely face death threats and violence. Teachers reported having to hide their Jewish identity or leave schools in Muslim-majority suburbs. After the murder of teacher Samuel Paty (2020) by an Islamist extremist, fear intensified. CRIF (the representative body of French Jewry) reported a sharp increase in threats against Jewish educators in 2024. Le Figaro documented cases of teachers who moved homes and changed their family names. The reality is stark: in 2024, Jewish teachers in the heart of Europe live in fear — a situation not seen since the 1930s.
Le Figaro, 2024 | CRIF Report 2024 | France 24 | The Local France

IDF Soldiers
Amos Yarkoni (Abd el-Majid Hidr)
A Bedouin Israeli who became the first commander of the IDF Minorities Battalion — symbol of partnership.
Amos Yarkoni (1920–1991), born Abd el-Majid Hidr, was an Israeli Bedouin who became one of the IDF's most senior non-Jewish officers. He founded and commanded the Minorities Battalion ("Unit 300") — the first IDF unit composed of Druze, Bedouins, and Circassians. Yarkoni participated in every Israeli war from Independence to the Six-Day War, in which he was severely wounded. He received the award for the IDF's most admired commander. His story represents the true partnership among all Israeli citizens — beyond religion and ethnicity.
IDF Archives | Israel Ministry of Defense | Ynet | Jewish Virtual Library

IDF Soldiers
Avigdor Kahalani
Tank commander who held the Valley of Tears (1973) with 7 tanks against 500 Syrian tanks.
Lieutenant Colonel Avigdor Kahalani commanded a tank battalion on the Golan Heights in the Yom Kippur War (1973). When Syria launched a surprise attack with approximately 500 tanks, Kahalani was left with only 7 operational tanks in what became known as the "Valley of Tears." For days, he and his soldiers held back the Syrian army and prevented a breakthrough that could have threatened the nation's existence. Kahalani himself was already severely burned from the Six-Day War (1967) — his face and hands were scarred when his tank was hit. His book "The Heights of Courage" describes the battles. He received the Medal of Valor, Israel's highest military decoration.
Avigdor Kahalani, "The Heights of Courage" (1984) | IDF Archives | Yad L'Shiryon Museum

IDF Soldiers
Major Roi Klein
Jumped on a grenade in the 2006 Lebanon War to save his soldiers — died reciting "Shema Yisrael."
Major Roi Klein, deputy commander of Battalion 51 in the Golani Brigade, fell on July 26, 2006, in a battle in Bint Jbeil, Lebanon, during the Second Lebanon War. When a grenade was thrown toward his group of soldiers, Roi jumped on the grenade and absorbed the explosion with his body to save their lives. Eyewitnesses reported that his last words were "Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad" ("Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One"). He was 31, a father of two. Klein was known as a beloved commander who cared for every soldier. He was posthumously awarded the Medal of Distinguished Service, and schools and streets were named after him.
IDF Archives | Israel Ministry of Defense | Ynet | Haaretz

IDF Soldiers
Druze Soldiers
~10% of Druze men serve in combat — highest per-capita casualty rate in the IDF.
Israel's Druze community (~150,000) has been conscripted into the IDF since 1956 — by agreement and at the request of community leaders. Approximately 10% of Druze men serve in combat units, and their per-capita casualty rate is among the highest in the IDF. Druze have served and continue to serve in all military branches, including elite units, and hundreds of Druze officers have reached senior ranks. The covenant of blood between the Druze and the State of Israel is one of the clearest examples of a true partnership of fate — and a living answer to the "apartheid state" accusation. The Druze defend Israel not because they must — but because they see it as home.
IDF Spokesperson | Israel CBS | Journal of Strategic Studies | Al-Monitor

Hostage Families
Noa Argamani
The iconic image of her kidnapping on a motorcycle — rescued in June 2024 in Operation Arnon.
Noa Argamani, 26, was kidnapped from the Nova festival on October 7, 2023. The video of her being taken on a Hamas motorcycle while screaming "Don't kill me!" became one of the most iconic images of that day — and a worldwide symbol of the hostages. Her father Yaakov and her boyfriend Avinatan Or fought tirelessly for her release. On June 8, 2024, Noa was rescued in Operation Arnon — a complex military operation in Nuseirat during which 4 living hostages were rescued. The moment Noa embraced her father became one of the most emotional moments in Israeli history. Tragically, her mother Liora, who had been diagnosed with brain cancer, passed away shortly after her return.
IDF Spokesperson, June 2024 | BBC | CNN | Times of Israel

American-Israeli, age 23 — his mother Rachel became a symbol of the struggle. Murdered in captivity August 2024.
Hersh Goldberg-Polin, a 23-year-old American-Israeli, was kidnapped from the Nova festival on October 7. While hiding in a shelter with friends, a grenade severed part of his arm. Kfir and Ariel Bibas
10 months old and 4 years old when kidnapped with their mother Shiri — the youngest hostages.
Kfir Bibas, 10 months old, and his brother Ariel, 4, were kidnapped on October 7, 2023, from Kibbutz Nir Oz along with their mother Shiri and father Yarden. Hostages & Missing Families Forum
200,000+ Israelis protesting weekly at Hostages Square — the largest protest movement in history.
The Hostages and Missing Families Forum was established days after October 7, 2023, as an organization of the families of 251 hostages. The Forum quickly became the largest civic protest movement in Israeli history. Every Saturday, over 200,000 Israelis gathered at "Hostages Square" (Museum Square in Tel Aviv) to demand the hostages' release. The Forum also operated internationally — families met with the US President, heads of state, the Pope, and the UN. The empty table with chairs in the square — symbolizing "we're saving your seat" — became a symbol of all the people of Israel.
Hostages & Missing Families Forum | Haaretz | BBC | Reuters
