Glossary

Precise definitions of terms everyone uses — but not everyone understands

Last updated: March 2026

Anti-Zionism (Anti-Zionism)

Opposition to the Jewish people's right to self-determination in their historical homeland. There is a fundamental difference between legitimate criticism of Israeli policy — which is a democratic right — and the denial of the basic right of Jews to a state. According to the IHRA definition, denying the Jewish people their right to self-determination (for example, claiming that the existence of the State of Israel is a racist endeavor) is an example of antisemitism. When only Jews are demanded to give up their state — that is discrimination, not criticism.

Antisemitism (Antisemitism)

Hatred, prejudice, and discrimination against Jews as individuals or as a collective. The most widely accepted working definition is the IHRA (International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance) definition, adopted in 2016 by 47 countries and 555 entities. It includes 11 practical examples. Importantly: the word "antisemitism" refers specifically to Jews — it was coined in 1879 by Wilhelm Marr to give a "scientific" veneer to Jew-hatred. The claim that "Arabs are also Semites" is a linguistic deflection that attempts to empty the term of its meaning.

Apartheid (Apartheid)

A regime of racial segregation that was practiced in South Africa (1948–1991), where laws separated whites from blacks in every domain — education, housing, transportation, and marriage. Applying this term to Israel is a misuse: in Israel, Arab citizens vote, serve in the Knesset (parliament), sit as Supreme Court justices, study at universities, and work as doctors in hospitals. Numerous international law experts (including Prof. Alan Dershowitz and Prof. Eugene Kontorovich) reject the apartheid comparison as legally inaccurate.

BDS (Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions)

A movement founded in 2005 calling for boycotts, divestment, and sanctions against Israel. Its founder, Omar Barghouti, admitted in an interview that the movement's goal is not policy change but the end of Israel's existence as a Jewish state. Germany defined BDS as antisemitic (Bundestag resolution, 2019). The movement focuses exclusively on Israel while ignoring countries with far more severe human rights violations — a classic double standard that is one of the hallmarks of antisemitism according to Sharansky's 3D Test.

Delegitimization (Delegitimization)

A systematic effort to deny the State of Israel's right to exist. Natan Sharansky developed the "3D Test" to identify when criticism of Israel crosses the line into antisemitism: Demonization — comparing Israel to the Nazis; Double Standards — demanding standards of Israel that are not demanded of any other nation; and Delegitimization — denying the right to exist. If criticism meets any of these criteria — it is antisemitism.

IHRA Definition (IHRA Definition)

The working definition of antisemitism formulated by the International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance (IHRA) and adopted in 2016. To date, 47 countries and 555 entities (municipalities, universities, organizations) have adopted it. The definition includes 11 practical examples, among them: Holocaust denial, accusing Jews of dual loyalty, applying double standards to Israel, and comparing Israeli policy to the Nazis. The definition is not a law — it is a working tool for identifying antisemitism.

Holocaust Denial (Holocaust Denial)

The denial or minimization of the Holocaust — the murder of six million Jews by Nazi Germany and its collaborators (1941–1945). Holocaust denial is illegal in more than 20 countries, including Germany, France, Austria, and Israel. Common tactics include: denying the existence of gas chambers, claiming the numbers are "inflated," presenting the Holocaust as "Zionist propaganda," and refusing to acknowledge the intentionality of the genocide. Holocaust denial is not an "opinion" — it is a documented lie whose purpose is to pave the way for renewed Jew-hatred.

Incitement / Demonization (Incitement / Demonization)

The use of degrading and dehumanizing language to render Jews or Israelis as subhuman in the eyes of the public. Historical and modern examples: the blood libel (Jews drink the blood of children), accusations of organ harvesting, comparing Israel or the IDF to Nazis, and slurs such as "baby killers." Demonization is an early stage in the process of genocide — according to Gregory Stanton's Ten Stages of Genocide (updated model, 2012), classification and dehumanization always precede physical violence.

Intifada (Intifada)

In Arabic: "shaking off." A term describing two Palestinian uprisings: the First Intifada (1987–1993) consisted mainly of demonstrations, strikes, and stone-throwing. The Second Intifada (2000–2005) was far more violent — it included suicide bombings inside Israeli cities, shootings, and killed more than 1,000 Israelis. Today, the call to "Globalize the Intifada" is heard at protests around the world — in practice, this is a call for violence against Jews.

Ethnic Cleansing (Ethnic Cleansing)

The organized removal of a population on the basis of ethnicity or religion from a given area. The term is misapplied to Israel — while the Palestinian population has grown by approximately 400% since 1967 (from about 1 million to more than 5 million in the West Bank and Gaza). By contrast: the Jewish population in Arab countries declined from 850,000 in 1948 to fewer than 4,000 today — a decrease of 99.5%. That is actual ethnic cleansing that most of the world ignores.

Human Shields (Human Shields)

Using civilians as a shield for military targets — a war crime under Article 51(7) of Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions. Hamas employs this strategy in a well-documented manner: firing rockets from schools, hospitals, and mosques; tunnels beneath residential neighborhoods; and storing weapons in civilian buildings. The UN itself reported finding rockets in UNRWA schools in Gaza. When civilians are harmed, Hamas presents it as an "Israeli crime" — while they are the ones who turned the civilians into targets.

Nakba (Nakba)

"Catastrophe" in Arabic — the Palestinian term for the events of 1948. Approximately 700,000 Palestinians were displaced or fled during the War of Independence. The context that is usually omitted: on November 29, 1947, the UN proposed a partition plan (Resolution 181) that would have established a Palestinian state alongside a Jewish state. The Jews accepted; the Arabs rejected it and launched a war. Five Arab nations (Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, and Lebanon) invaded Israel on the day of its declaration of independence. The "Nakba" would not have occurred had the partition plan not been rejected and the Arab invasion not taken place.

Status Quo (Status Quo)

The existing arrangement at the holy sites, particularly the Temple Mount / Al-Aqsa in Jerusalem. Under the agreement, Jordan (through the Waqf) is responsible for administering the Temple Mount; Muslims pray there; Jews are permitted to ascend the Mount but are prohibited from praying (despite it being the holiest site in Judaism). Jews pray only at the Western Wall. The claim that "Israel threatens Al-Aqsa" has been recurring since 1929 (the Hebron massacre) and has repeatedly served as incitement to violence — despite Israel consistently maintaining the status quo.

Blood Libel (Blood Libel)

A false accusation that Jews use the blood of non-Jewish children for religious purposes (such as baking matzah). The libel was first documented in Norwich, England, in 1144, and since then approximately 150 cases have been recorded across Europe throughout history. Blood libels led to pogroms, expulsions, and mass murders. In modern versions, the libel appears as accusations of Israel "harvesting organs" from Palestinians (an article in Sweden's Aftonbladet, 2009) or depictions of Israel as "drinking children's blood" in cartoons in the Arab press.

Pogrom (Pogrom)

Violent riots directed against Jews — involving murder, destruction of property, and rape. The word originates from Russian ("to destroy"). The major pogroms began in 1881 in Russia following the assassination of Tsar Alexander II, and the Kishinev pogrom (1903) shocked the world. Pogroms occurred throughout Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa. On October 7, 2023, the massacre carried out by Hamas was described by numerous historians as "the largest pogrom against Jews since World War II."

Protocols of the Elders of Zion (Protocols of the Elders of Zion)

A fabricated document (published in 1903 in Russia) that purports to document a "Jewish plan for world domination." It was proven to be a forgery as early as 1921 by the British newspaper The Times — the text was copied from an 1864 French satire entirely unrelated to Jews. Nevertheless, the Nazis used it as propaganda, and Hamas cites it in their charter (Article 32). The Protocols are still distributed in the Arab world and Eastern Europe as though they are authentic — they are the most influential antisemitic document in history.

Israel Defense Forces (IDF — Israel Defense Forces)

The Israel Defense Forces, established in 1948 from the merger of pre-state defense organizations (Haganah, Irgun, Lehi). The IDF is a conscription army — both men and women are drafted (with exemptions for minorities and others). The IDF's core principle is "Purity of Arms" — an ethical doctrine requiring the use of force only against military targets, prevention of civilian casualties to the greatest extent possible, and moral conduct even on the battlefield. The IDF warns enemy civilians before strikes (leaflets, "roof knocking," phone calls) — Israel developed this method, which was also adopted by the U.S. Air Force from 2016 onward (in Mosul, Iraq).

Zionism (Zionism)

The national liberation movement of the Jewish people — the right to self-determination in their historical homeland. The political movement was founded by Theodor Herzl in 1897 at the First Zionist Congress in Basel, Switzerland, but the Jewish connection to the Land of Israel spans 3,000+ years. Zionism achieved its goal in 1948 with the establishment of the State of Israel. Zionism is not racism — it is a national movement, like any other national liberation movement. There are Zionists of all races, religions, and genders.

Genocide (Genocide)

According to the UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (1948), genocide requires dolus specialis — the specific intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group. This is a precise legal definition, not a metaphor. The casual use of the word "genocide" empties it of meaning and trivializes the victims of actual genocide — the Holocaust, Rwanda, Armenia. When the Palestinian population is growing at one of the highest rates in the world, the claim of "genocide" contradicts both the legal definition and the demographic facts.

Hostage (Hostage)

A person held in captivity by force as a bargaining chip. On October 7, 2023, Hamas abducted 251 people — infants, children, women, elderly, and civilians from approximately 25 countries — and held them in Gaza. Taking hostages is a war crime under the Geneva Conventions (1949) and additional protocols. Hamas refused to allow Red Cross visits, did not provide information about their condition, and used them as leverage. The abduction of civilians — including children and infants — is not "resistance" but terrorism by every definition in international law.
