🧬 Science Says: One People

Findings from research in genetics, epigenetics, and social psychology support the model of biological and cultural connection between parts of the community.

🧬 Genetic Evidence: We Are Closer to Each Other Than to Our Neighbors

A groundbreaking study by Atzmon et al. (2010) in the American Journal of Human Genetics analyzed 652,000 genetic variants in 237 Jews from 7 communities (Iranian, Iraqi, Syrian, Italian, Turkish, Greek, Ashkenazi). The result: all Jewish communities share ancient Middle Eastern ancestors and are genetically more similar to each other than to their non-Jewish neighbors.

An additional study by Behar et al. (2010) in Nature confirmed: most Jews worldwide are descendants of ancient Levantine populations.

Atzmon et al., AJHG 86(6):850-859, 2010 | Behar et al., Nature 466:238-242, 2010

🧬 Epigenetic Evidence: We Carry the Same Scars

Dr. Rachel Yehuda of the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai studied 32 Holocaust survivors and their descendants. She found epigenetic changes in the FKBP5 gene (a gene that regulates stress response) — in both survivors and their children. This was the first time pre-parental trauma's epigenetic impact on the next generation was demonstrated.

We don't just share history — we carry it in our bodies.

Yehuda et al., Biological Psychiatry 80(5):372-380, 2016

🧠 Psychological Evidence: The Strongest Group Bond in History

Psychologist Henri Tajfel proved in 1971 that even arbitrary group division (based on painting preferences!) creates in-group loyalty. Now imagine a group with 3,500 years of shared memory, shared language, shared persecution. The Jewish group bond is among the strongest ever documented.

Tajfel et al., European Journal of Social Psychology 1(2):149-177, 1971
