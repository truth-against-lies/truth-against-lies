06 The Halakha — "Captives Shall Not Be Ransomed for More Than Their Worth"

Pidyon Shvuyim (Redemption of Captives) is considered one of the greatest commandments in Judaism. Maimonides ruled: "Whoever turns a blind eye to redeeming captives transgresses 'Do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor.'"

But the Talmud establishes a crucial exception (Gittin 45a):

"Captives shall not be ransomed for more than their worth — for the sake of Tikkun Olam (repairing the world)."
— Babylonian Talmud, Tractate Gittin 45a

The reason: excessive ransom encourages further kidnappings — "Tikkun Olam" = preventing greater future harm.

Rabbi Meir of Rothenburg (13th century) — one of the greatest halakhic authorities — was captured by the German Emperor. He refused to allow his own ransom, arguing that an excessive ransom would encourage further kidnapping of rabbis. He died in captivity after 7 years.

⚠ Israel's deals violate the halakhic principle: 1,027 for one (Shalit), 4,830 for six (PLO) — this far exceeds "their worth." The halakhic prediction proved correct: kidnappings escalated, and October 7 proved that Sinwar planned his entire attack around taking hostages — precisely because he knew it works.

07 The Shamgar Commission — Recommendations No Government Adopted

In 2012, following the Shalit deal, the Shamgar Commission was established under former Supreme Court President Meir Shamgar.

The commission's key recommendations:

1.Transfer responsibility for captives/MIAs from the Prime Minister's Office to the Ministry of Defense
2.Release only a limited number of prisoners per captive
3.For dead captives: exchange bodies for bodies only — not living terrorists for bodies
⚠ The recommendations remained classified. No Israeli government formally adopted them — 14 years and 255 hostages later (as of March 2026). Every new government repeats the same pattern: releasing thousands of terrorists under public pressure.
