🔬 Da'at vs. Dat

A linguistic and philosophical analysis of the difference between Da'at (experiential knowledge) and Dat (institutional framework), through letter analysis, gematria, and Kabbalistic sources

❝ Know the God of your father and serve Him with a whole heart and a willing soul ❞ 1 Chronicles 28:9

  • 22 Hebrew letters = divine tools of creation. "Emet" (truth) stands firm, "Sheker" (lie) wobbles

🔬 The Hebrew Language — A Linguistic and Spiritual Structure

The Hebrew language is characterized by a unique connection between form, sound, numerical value, and meaning. According to the Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Creation), the 22 letters and 10 Sefirot are the foundations of creation. Analysis of the letters' structure reveals layers of meaning that do not exist in other languages.

22 Letters = 22 Building Blocks of Reality

According to the Sefer Yetzirah, twenty-two foundational letters are the tools through which everything was created. In the mystical understanding, these are creative forces, not merely metaphor. "Twenty-two foundational letters... He engraved them, carved them, combined them, weighed them, interchanged them, and formed through them all of creation."

Sefer Yetzirah 2:2

Each Letter: Form, Sound, and Number

According to tradition, the form of each letter reflects its meaning: Aleph (1) symbolizes a person pointing to heaven and earth; Bet (2) symbolizes an open house; Gimel (3) symbolizes a person giving. In the Kabbalistic understanding, this is intentional architecture, not arbitrary convention.

Otiot de-Rabbi Akiva; Tanya, Sha'ar HaYichud VehaEmunah, Chapter 1

Gematria — The Numerical Dimension

Every Hebrew letter has a fixed numerical value (Aleph=1, Bet=2... Yud=10, Kaf=20... Kuf=100...). Words with the same numerical sum are considered semantically linked. This method is not numerology but a systematic hermeneutic tool, used in the Talmud and by classical commentators to uncover additional layers of meaning in the text.

Babylonian Talmud, multiple tractates; Ramban on the Torah

Words Reveal Truth on Their Own

A classic example: "Sheker" (lie — Shin-Kuf-Resh) — each letter stands on one leg (an unstable structure). "Emet" (truth — Aleph-Mem-Tav) — each letter stands on a broad base (a stable structure). According to the Talmud, the structure of the letters itself encodes the idea that truth is stable and falsehood lacks foundation.

Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 104a

The Power of Speech

According to the biblical narrative, creation was done through speech — "And God said." In Jewish thought, speech is not merely communication but an act of creation. Based on this principle, the severity of the prohibition against lashon hara (evil speech) is explained: words, in this understanding, create reality.

Tanya, Sha'ar HaYichud VehaEmunah; Chofetz Chaim, Shmirat HaLashon

✨ Da'at — The Hidden Sefirah

The word "Da'at" in Kabbalistic thought describes a form of knowledge deeper than intellectual recognition — an experiential knowledge that transforms the knower's very essence. In the structure of the Sefirot, Da'at is positioned as the connector between Chochmah (intuition) and Binah (analysis).

"And Adam knew Eve his wife"

The first use of the root D-A-T (to know) in the Torah is "And Adam knew Eve his wife" (Genesis 4:1). According to classical commentary, this is not intellectual knowledge but essential union. This usage defines the concept of Da'at: knowing through merging with the known, as opposed to theoretical knowledge.

Rashi and Ramban on Genesis 4:1

The Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil

The tree in the Garden of Eden is called "the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil." According to commentary, Adam and Eve did not acquire theoretical information about good and evil but experienced them in a way that changed their essence. Thus the power of Da'at is revealed: it does not leave the knower as they were.

Ramchal, Derech Hashem 1:3; Zohar, Bereishit

Da'at in the Sefirot — The Hidden Bridge

In the Tree of Life there are 10 Sefirot. But Da'at is sometimes counted and sometimes not — it is the 11th, the hidden Sefirah. When Keter (will/supreme crown) is too transcendent to grasp, Da'at serves as its revealed face. It is the bridge between the intellectual Sefirot (Chochmah and Binah) and the emotional Sefirot (Chesed through Yesod). Without it — there is no connection between thought and emotion.

Etz Chaim, Rabbi Chaim Vital; Tanya, Chapter 3

Da'at = Connection

"Da'at — is a language of attachment and bonding, that one bonds their mind with a strong and mighty bond, and fixes their thought firmly on the greatness of the Infinite One, blessed be He"

This is how the author of the Tanya defines Da'at: not to know about God — but to know through connection to God. Da'at is not information — it is attachment. Like the difference between reading about fire and feeling the heat.

Tanya, Likutei Amarim, Chapter 3

When Da'at Is Absent

"Every person who has Da'at — it is as if the Temple was built in their days. Every person who lacks Da'at — it is as if the Temple was destroyed in their days"

The Talmud equates Da'at with the building of the Temple. Without Da'at — without genuine inner connection — external structures of knowledge remain empty, like a Temple that stands intact on the outside but is destroyed within.

Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 92a; Berakhot 33a

📜 Dat — A Word That Changed

The word "Dat" in modern Hebrew means "religion." But the original meaning was entirely different. Understanding the evolution of this word reveals one of the deepest tensions in Jewish history: between living spiritual connection and institutional structure.

Biblical Period

The Biblical Meaning of "Dat"

In the Bible, the word "Dat" appears mainly in the Book of Esther and the Book of Daniel. Its meaning there: law, decree, royal edict. "Ke-dat" = according to the law. "Ve-dat nitna be-Shushan" — a formula for a law that was issued. The word referred to decrees and legal rules — not to spiritual belief or religious practice.

Esther 1:8, 3:8, 4:16; Daniel 6:6, 6:13
Middle Ages

How "Dat" Became "Religion"

In the Middle Ages, under the influence of Arabic ("din" = religion) and through contact with Christianity and Islam, the Hebrew word "Dat" underwent a semantic shift and came to denote "religion" in the institutional sense: a system of beliefs, rituals, and organizational authority. This is a post-biblical innovation. The original biblical word did not refer at all to what we today call "religion."

Prof. Aviezer Ravitzky, Religion and State in Jewish Philosophy; Even-Shoshan Dictionary
The Eternal Problem

The Problem: Dat Without Da'at

"This people draw near with their mouth, and with their lips do honor Me — but have removed their heart far from Me"

When Dat (framework) disconnects from Da'at (experiential knowledge), it becomes an empty shell. The prophets cried out against exactly this: external observance without inner connection. Maimonides explains that this is precisely the reason idolatry developed — people forgot the "why" and were left only with the "what."

Isaiah 29:13; Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed 3:32
Maimonides' Warning

Rote Commandments vs. Knowledge of God

Maimonides (1138–1204) warned that most people observe religion out of habit — "commandments learned by rote." He insisted that the purpose of all commandments is to arrive at "knowledge of God" — a true knowing of the Divine. Without intellectual and experiential understanding, the ritual is meaningless. "The purpose of all the commandments is that you should know your Creator."

Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed 3:51; Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah 2:2

⚖ The Critical Difference — Da'at as Experience, Dat as Framework

The analysis is not directed against Dat as a framework, but rather in favor of integrating Da'at within the religious framework. In the Kabbalistic metaphor: a framework (Dat) without inner content (Da'at) lacks meaning, and inner content without a framework lacks a vessel for expression. The ideal is the integration of both.

Dat says: "Do because you must." Da'at says: "Understand why, and do because you know."

The Zohar distinguishes between an "eved" (servant who acts from fear) and a "ben" (son who acts from love and understanding). Both follow the same Torah — but the inner experience is entirely different. The servant observes Dat. The son acts from Da'at. The external result is identical, but the inner person is completely different.

Zohar 3:152a; Tanya, Chapters 41–42

Dat creates conformity. Da'at creates inner transformation.

When Judaism is reduced to external compliance alone — what to eat, when to pray, how to dress — it produces social conformity but not necessarily spiritual growth. When commandments are understood as a gateway to inner connection, they create fundamental change in the person.

Ramchal, Mesillat Yesharim, Introduction; Tanya, Likutei Amarim, Chapter 5

Dat divides people. Da'at unites them.

Institutional religion creates in-groups and out-groups: "Our denomination vs. theirs." But Da'at — genuine knowledge — reveals that all of Israel (and ultimately all of humanity) share one source. "Israel, the Torah, and the Holy One blessed be He — are all one." This unity is revealed through Da'at, not through institutional affiliation.

Zohar 2:60b; Tanya, Chapter 32

The Ideal: Dat Filled with Da'at

The goal is not to abandon Dat. The goal is to fill it with knowledge. A mitzvah performed with intention and understanding is incomparably more powerful than one performed mechanically. The Baal Shem Tov revolutionized Judaism by insisting that even the simplest Jew, who prays with a full heart, reaches higher than the greatest scholar who prays out of routine.

Baal Shem Tov, Tzava'at HaRivash; Toldot Yaakov Yosef

🔭 Letter Analysis — Dalet, Ayin, Tav

The two words — Da'at and Dat — share two letters: Dalet and Tav. The difference between them is a single letter: Ayin. This one letter holds the entire secret.

ד

Dalet = The Door

Dalet means "door." Its numerical value is 4 — representing the four directions of the world, the physical realm. Both Dat and Da'at begin with Dalet — with a door. The question is: Does the door open to a narrow corridor (Dat without depth) or to infinite space (Da'at)? The Zohar says Dalet is the letter of the poor person (dal) standing at the entrance, asking to be filled.

Zohar, Introduction (Otiot de-Rabbi Akiva); Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 104a
ע

Ayin = The Eye / The Spring

This is the letter that separates Da'at from Dat. Ayin means "eye" (sight/perception) and also "spring" (source of living water). Its numerical value is 70 — seventy facets of the Torah, seventy nations, the fullness of perspectives. When Ayin is present — there is sight: deep perception, multiple viewpoints, inner vision. When Ayin is removed — there is Dat without eyes: blind religion.

Sefer Yetzirah; Baal HaTurim on the Torah; Zohar on 70 facets of the Torah
ת

Tav = The Mark / Truth / Completion

Tav is the last letter in the Hebrew alphabet — representing completion, wholeness, and a seal of truth (the Talmud says God's seal is "emet" — truth, which ends with Tav). Both Da'at and Dat end with Tav. Both seek truth and completion. But Da'at reaches it through sight (Ayin), while Dat attempts to reach it through framework alone.

Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 55a; Yoma 69b

The Equation: Dat + Ayin (Sight) = Da'at

Mathematically: Dat + Ayin = Da'at. Religion + Sight = Knowledge. This is not coincidence. The Hebrew language encodes this teaching: take religion, add inner vision, and you arrive at true knowledge. Remove the sight — and you have a blind framework. The letters themselves teach the way.

Multiple Kabbalistic commentaries on letter combinations

🌳 The Tree of Life — Da'at's Place

The Kabbalistic Tree of Life (the Sefirotic Tree) maps the structure of divine emanation. Understanding Da'at's place in this map reveals why it is called "the hidden Sefirah" and why it is the key to all spiritual work.

10 Sefirot — A Brief Map

The ten Sefirot are: Keter (supreme will), Chochmah (intuitive flash), Binah (analytical understanding), Chesed (giving), Gevurah (restriction), Tiferet (harmony), Netzach (victory), Hod (acknowledgment), Yesod (connection), Malkhut (physical reality). Da'at is the hidden 11th — sometimes replacing Keter in the count, sometimes standing between Chochmah and Binah.

Etz Chaim, Rabbi Chaim Vital; Pardes Rimonim, Rabbi Moshe Cordovero

Why Da'at Is Hidden

Da'at is hidden because it represents the point of connection between the transcendent (Keter — the divine will beyond comprehension) and the accessible (Chochmah and Binah). It is the place where the Infinite meets the finite. One cannot "see" Da'at directly — one can only experience it as a moment of connection. Like a joint in a bone: invisible, but the entire body depends on it.

Tanya, Chapter 3; Etz Chaim, Sha'ar 23

Da'at and the Temple

The Holy of Holies in the Temple corresponds to Da'at in the Tree of Life. There stood the Ark of the Covenant, containing the Tablets. The Talmud says the Ark "was not of the measure" — a paradox of the infinite within the finite, exactly like Da'at. Only the High Priest entered, once a year, on Yom Kippur — a moment of absolute connection between the human and the divine.

Babylonian Talmud, Yoma 21a; Megillah 10b; Zohar on Terumah

The Shattering of the Vessels (Shevirat HaKelim)

In Lurianic Kabbalah, the primordial vessels intended to contain divine light shattered because they lacked Da'at — the ability to receive and integrate. The entire purpose of human existence is "tikkun" — repair — which means the repair of Da'at: the ability to contain divine light without shattering. Every mitzvah, every good deed, every moment of genuine understanding — is an act of restoring Da'at.

Etz Chaim, Sha'ar HaKlalim; Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto, Klalei Pitchei Chochmah

⚠ Dat Without Da'at — Practical Expressions

After presenting the theoretical framework, we can examine practical expressions of religious observance disconnected from critical thinking. The data is presented for analysis, not for judgment.

Charms and Commercialization of Faith

Red string — The belief that it protects from the evil eye is widespread. It should be noted that the Tosefta (Shabbat 7–8) explicitly prohibits this as "ways of the Amorites." This custom, marketed as Jewish tradition, contradicts the halakhic sources themselves.

Amulets and "holy water" — A market worth millions of shekels, including paid blessings, holy names on parchment, and similar products. Researchers point to the exploitation of religious anxiety for commercial purposes.

Tosefta Shabbat 7–8; Maimonides, Hilkhot Avodah Zarah 11:12

Maimonides' Position on Charms

"All these things are all words of falsehood and lies, and it is not fitting for Israel, who are wise and sagacious, to be drawn after these vanities, nor to entertain the thought that there is any benefit in them"

Maimonides ruled unequivocally. In Hilkhot Avodah Zarah Chapter 11, he prohibits: divination, sorcery, incantations, amulets, necromancy, and every form of "charm" not based on reason. In the Guide for the Perplexed (3:37) he explains that the purpose of the commandments is to distance a person from idolatry — and a return to charms constitutes, in his view, a regression from that very goal.

Maimonides, Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Avodah Zarah 11:12–16; Guide for the Perplexed 3:37

"Da'at Torah" — Evolution of the Concept

The origin (1914): Agudath Israel determined that on public issues, Torah sages would guide the community. This was a leadership concept intended to provide spiritual direction.

The development: The concept expanded into an expectation of absolute obedience to rabbinic leadership rulings in every domain, even without explanation or discussion. Scholars like Lawrence Kaplan point out that this development contradicts the principle of Da'at — knowledge through understanding.

Decisions of Moetzes Gedolei HaTorah, Agudath Israel 1914; Lawrence Kaplan, "Daas Torah: A Modern Conception of Rabbinic Authority" (1992)

The Oven of Akhnai — When the Torah Itself Says "Think for Yourself"

A famous story in the Talmud (Bava Metzia 59b): Rabbi Eliezer brought every possible proof for his position — including a heavenly voice. And the sages rejected it. Rabbi Yehoshua stood up and said: "It is not in heaven" — the Torah was already given to human beings, and we decide.

And what did God say? "My children have defeated Me, My children have defeated Me" — God Himself, according to the Talmud, rejoiced when human beings think for themselves.

Babylonian Talmud, Bava Metzia 59b

Restricting Access to Information

Kosher phones: Without internet, SMS only. The stated purpose: protection from immodest content. The practical result: restricting access to information, news, and general knowledge.

Censored press: Panim Chadashot, Yated Ne'eman — censorship of women's photos, filtering news that does not align with the editorial line, and presenting a one-sided viewpoint.

Key statistic: 84% of yeshiva students do not study secular core subjects — mathematics, English, or science. The data indicates that this is a factor in creating economic dependence.

Taub Center Report, "Haredim in the Labor Market" (2023); CBS Israel data

The Other Side — Benefits of Protection from Harmful Content

Real protection: The internet contains pornography, violence, gambling, and addictive content. Studies indicate severe harm to children and adolescents exposed without supervision. Maintaining a clean environment from such content is a legitimate moral value.

Preserving family values: Limiting exposure to destructive content strengthens the family structure and marriage stability. Divorce rates in the Haredi community are significantly lower than the national average.

Focus and learning: Without social media distractions, yeshiva students develop deep concentration abilities. Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg himself admitted that his products are addictive and harmful to mental health.

The real challenge: The question is not "whether to protect?" but "how to protect without blocking essential knowledge?" — it is possible to protect from pornography and gambling without blocking access to mathematics, science, and livelihood.

Rambam, Hilchot De'ot 6:1; Chafetz Chaim, Shemirat HaLashon; Mesillat Yesharim, Chapter 11

The Economic Consequences

33% of Haredim live below the poverty line (compared to 14% of the general population). An average family of 6.9 people with one or half a breadwinner. The data indicates dependence on government and communal systems.

According to the linguistic equation analyzed: Dalet + Ayin = Da'at. When Ayin (eye/sight) is removed — Dat without sight remains. The data indicates that the absence of core studies is linked to high poverty rates and economic limitations.

⚖ The Religious-Secular Divide — An Analysis

The divide between Haredim and secular Jews constitutes one of the most significant internal challenges of the Jewish people. Below is a data-based analysis of the roots of the divide and its expressions.

1893–1904

Herzl — The Man Behind the Myth

Lesser-known facts: Before becoming a Zionist, Herzl proposed in 1893 that Viennese Jews undergo mass conversion in St. Stephen's Cathedral. He abandoned the idea when he concluded that conversion would not solve antisemitism. His son, Hans Herzl, was not circumcised. Herzl envisioned a state where rabbis would be "locked in their synagogues" without political influence.

Context: Herzl grew up as an assimilated Jew in Vienna. His vision was secular-national. His critics point out that he tried to create a national framework without addressing the spiritual-religious dimension of Jewish identity.

Theodor Herzl, Diaries (1895–1904); Alex Bein, Theodor Herzl: A Biography (1934)
Ketubot 111a

The Three Oaths — The Argument That Divides the Nation

The argument: The Talmud relates that God imposed three oaths on Israel: not to ascend "as a wall" to the Land of Israel, not to rebel against the nations, and the nations would not persecute Israel excessively. Satmar and Neturei Karta use this as the basis for opposition to Zionism.

The counterargument: The Talmud does not rule the oaths as binding law. Maimonides does not cite them. Rav Kook argued that the Balfour Declaration (1917) = "permission of the nations" that nullifies the oath. And if the nations "violated" their oath (the Holocaust) — Israel too is exempt.

Babylonian Talmud, Ketubot 111a; Rav Abraham Isaac Kook, Letters; Rabbi Avigdor Miller, Dissent
Extremists

Satmar, Neturei Karta, and "The Holocaust as Punishment"

Satmar: Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum (1887–1979) wrote "Vayoel Moshe" — the ideological basis for the position that Zionism constitutes rebellion against God, and that the Holocaust is a result of Zionism. This position is controversial even among most Haredim.

Neturei Karta: A fringe group of approximately 5,000 members. Members participated in the Holocaust denial conference in Tehran (2006), met with Arafat, and share a platform with Hamas. It should be noted that the majority of the Haredi community disavows them.

Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum, Vayoel Moshe (1961); BBC coverage of Tehran conference (2006)
1948–2024

Military Service — From 400 to 62,500

1948: Ben-Gurion exempted 400 yeshiva students from military service. The rationale: the world of Torah was nearly destroyed in the Holocaust; what remained needed to be preserved. An understandable decision in its historical context.

2010: The number swelled to 62,500 exemptions. What was a rare exception became the norm for 13% of the Jewish population.

2024: The Supreme Court ruled there is no longer a legal basis for the exemption. The issue remains contentious.

The dilemma: The Haredi claim that "Torah study protects" stands against the secular claim about an unequal distribution of the service burden. Both claims reflect different value systems. The challenge is finding a solution acceptable to both sides.

Ben-Gurion Decision 1948; Supreme Court HCJ 1877/14 (2024); CBS Israel data
Pew 2016

The Numbers Don't Lie

The Pew Survey (2016) revealed the depth of the divide:

• 43% of secular Jews said Haredim are "a burden on society"

• Only 12% of Haredim said that about secular Jews

• 44% of secular Jews said they would not want their child to marry a Haredi person

• ~13% of Israeli Jews identify as Haredi — with a growth rate of 4% per year

These figures reflect significant perception gaps between sectors of the Jewish population in Israel.

Pew Research Center, "Israel's Religiously Divided Society" (March 2016)

🅍 The Bridge — Unifying Factors

The divide is real, yet unifying factors also exist. It is possible to identify areas where each side contributes to the other, and models demonstrating successful integration.

What Secular Jews Can Learn from Haredim

Community: The Haredi structure of mutual responsibility is exemplary. Gemachim (thousands in Israel), Hatzalah (volunteer EMS with a 2.5-minute response time!), Shomrim (security patrols), Chevra Kadisha. When a Haredi person falls — the community catches them. In the secular world there is no equivalent.

Family: 6.9 children on average. One can debate the economics, but the commitment to family, to marriage, and to raising children — is something the secular Western world is losing.

Shabbat: A compulsory weekly gathering. No phones. Eating together. Rich and poor in the same synagogue. No secular institution has managed to replicate the social power of Shabbat.

What Haredim Can Learn from Secular Jews

Earning a dignified living: The Torah itself says "Torah study is excellent together with a worldly occupation" (Avot 2:2). Maimonides worked as a physician. Rashi was a vintner. Rabbi Yehoshua was a charcoal maker. Work is not a contradiction to Torah — it is part of it.

Science: "Wisdom among the nations — believe it" (Eichah Rabbah 2:13). Studying science, mathematics, and languages is not a threat to faith — it expands Da'at (!) and enables earning a living.

Service: "If there is no flour — there is no Torah" (Avot 3:17). And "if there is no security — there is no Torah" either. Those who guard the borders make Torah study possible.

Examples of Success — The Bridge Is Already Being Built

Netzah Yehuda (Nahal Haredi): Thousands of Haredim serving in the IDF within a framework that maintains kashrut and prayer. Proving that both are possible.

Haredi Hi-Tech: A growing industry. Companies like KamaTech bridge the hi-tech world and the Haredi community. Thousands of Haredim in QA, development, and data science.

Pre-army preparatory programs: Religious-Zionists who study Torah and serve. The model proving it can be done.

Mizrahi communities: In many Sephardic communities there is no sharp divide between "religious" and "secular." Grandmother keeps Shabbat, the grandchild doesn't, and they still eat together on Friday. Unity through family, not through ideology.

The Message — Unity Does Not Mean Uniformity

Unity does not require uniformity. It is possible to recognize a plurality of paths while maintaining shared identity.

From an external perspective, there is no distinction between Haredi and secular — hostile forces see all Jews as one entity. This fact underscores the importance of internal unity.

The Talmud says: "All of Israel are guarantors for one another" (Sanhedrin 27b). Not "all Haredim" or "all secular Jews." All of Israel.

Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 27b; Shevuot 39a

🛡 From Dat to Da'at — The Practical Path

Intellectual recognition of the difference between Da'at and Dat is itself "Dat" (information only). The question is how to transform theoretical knowledge into "Da'at" (living experience). Below are practical steps grounded in sources.

🛠 6 Steps from Dat to Da'at

  1. Learn through questions, not just answers — The entire Talmud is built on questions. "Mai ka mashma lan?" (What does this teach us?). Never accept learning passively. Ask: Why? What is the deeper layer? Da'at comes from active engagement, not passive reception.
  2. Pray with intention, even a single sentence — It is better to say one blessing with full concentration than an entire prayer book on autopilot. The Baal Shem Tov taught: one word spoken with a burning heart reaches higher than a thousand pages read with a cold heart.
  3. Study the Hebrew letters — Each letter is a meditation. Explore the forms, the meanings, the numerical values. Start with the Sefer Yetzirah. The letters are gates — whoever studies them begins to see the world differently.
  4. Find a Rav (teacher) — Da'at cannot be fully transmitted through books. The Mishnah says: "Appoint for yourself a teacher" (Avot 1:6). A living teacher embodies knowledge and can transmit it from person to person, from soul to soul.
  5. Practice contemplation — The Tanya instructs regular contemplation: deep meditation on the greatness of the Creator, the structure of creation, the meaning of your soul. This is the Chabad practice that transforms Dat into Da'at — not just to know, but to feel.
  6. Live it — The ultimate Da'at is not in the head. It is in action. "It is not the study that is essential but the deed" (Avot 1:17). But a deed performed with understanding (Da'at) is incomparably more powerful than a deed performed out of habit (Dat alone).

Guiding principles:
Not to settle for observance without understanding.
To seek the "why" behind every "what."
To see with the eye of Ayin — in depth, from 70 angles, with inner vision.
To aspire to integrate Da'at within the framework of Dat.
Deep understanding grants religious observance meaning and power.
