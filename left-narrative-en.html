📜 The Historical Pattern

Throughout Jewish history, there are documented cases of Jews who acted against the collective interest — sometimes out of fear, sometimes out of ideology, and sometimes out of personal interest. Below are documented cases.

~167 BCE

The Hellenizers

Jews who adopted Greek culture and assisted Antiochus IV in desecrating the Temple. According to the sources, they cooperated with the conqueror and supported decrees that harmed Jewish worship. The process of Hellenization threatened the continuity of Jewish identity until the Maccabean revolt.

Sources: 1 Maccabees, Chapters 1-2; Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews, Book 12
Middle Ages

The Informers and Apostates

Apostate Jews who informed the Church against their brethren. Nicholas Donin (13th century) — a Jew who converted to Christianity and caused the burning of the Talmud in Paris in 1242 (24 cartloads of manuscripts). Pablo de Santa Maria (formerly Solomon HaLevi, 1352-1435) — persecuted his community in Burgos after converting and becoming a bishop.

Sources: Heinrich Graetz, History of the Jews; William C. Jordan, The French Monarchy and the Jews (1989)
1918-1930

The Yevsektsiya — Jews Who Destroyed Judaism

Jewish Communists who established the "Jewish Section" of the Communist Party. Under Semyon Dimanstein's leadership, the Section closed synagogues, yeshivas, cheders, and Zionist movements, and persecuted religious leaders. At a later stage, Stalin eliminated most of the Section's members themselves (1948-1953).

Sources: Zvi Gitelman, A Century of Ambivalence (2001); Anna Shternshis, Soviet and Kosher (2006)
1940s

The Judenrat — An Extremely Complex Subject

Jewish councils that the Nazis forced to establish in the ghettos. Adam Czerniakow in Warsaw — committed suicide in 1942 when he refused to sign deportation lists for children to Treblinka. Conversely, Mordechai Chaim Rumkowski in Lodz cooperated in the hope of saving a "remnant" — and was himself sent to Auschwitz.

Important: One should not easily judge people who faced impossible dilemmas under a murderous regime. The subject is deeply contested among historians.

Sources: Isaiah Trunk, Judenrat (1972); Hannah Arendt, Eichmann in Jerusalem (1963)
1944-1957

Rudolf Kastner — Savior or Traitor?

A Hungarian Jew who negotiated with Eichmann. He saved 1,685 Jews on the "Kastner Train" to Switzerland. But he was accused of knowing about the extermination plan and concealing the information from Hungarian Jewry. The Gruenwald-Kastner trial (1954) tore Israel apart. Kastner was assassinated in Tel Aviv in 1957.

Two sides: Some historians view him as a hero who saved as many as possible. Others — a collaborator who sacrificed the many for the few.

Sources: Kastner-Gruenwald Trial (1954-1958); Anna Porter, Kasztner's Train (2007)

🧠 The Psychology — Analyzing the Phenomenon

Why do Jews sometimes act against Jewish interests? Researchers in social psychology, sociology, and psychoanalysis have examined the phenomenon from different angles.

Identification with the Aggressor

When a person feels powerless before a threatening force, they may internalize the aggressor's positions as a psychological defense mechanism. Instead of feeling fear toward the attacker, they adopt their perspective.

Example: Jews in hostile academic environments may adopt anti-Zionist narratives as a means of reducing social pressure and gaining acceptance in their surroundings.

Sources: Anna Freud, The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defence (1936); Sandor Ferenczi, International Journal of Psycho-Analysis (1949)

"Court Jew" Syndrome

A historical phenomenon in which a Jew receives special status under a ruler's patronage, sometimes at the expense of their community. In the modern context: Jewish academics, journalists, and intellectuals who gain legitimacy in anti-Zionist circles through criticism of Israel.

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks wrote: "The anti-Zionist narrative provides certain Jews with a way to be accepted in societies that would otherwise reject them."

Sources: Selma Stern, The Court Jew (1950); Jonathan Sacks, "The Mutating Virus" (2016)

Universalism That Erases Particularism

An ideology that views any national or ethnic belonging as outdated "tribalism." Jews who adopt extreme universalism cease to see themselves as part of a people with legitimate needs — and begin to see Israel as an "obstacle" to their global vision.

Source: Alvin Rosenfeld, Progressive Jewish Thought and the New Anti-Semitism (AJC, 2006)

Jewish Self-Hatred

The term was defined by Theodor Lessing in his book Der jüdische Selbsthass (1930). Not simple "self-hatred" — but the internalization of antisemitic stereotypes as part of an acculturation process. The Jew wants to be accepted, and therefore emphasizes the "differences" between themselves and "other Jews."

Sources: Theodor Lessing, Der jüdische Selbsthass (1930); Sander Gilman, Jewish Self-Hatred (1986)

The "As-a-Jew" Phenomenon

Using Jewish identity as legitimation for anti-Jewish positions. "As a Jew, I oppose Israel" — the identity serves as a shield against criticism ("you can't call me antisemitic, I'm Jewish") and as a weapon ("even Jews agree").

David Hirsh described this as the "Livingstone Formulation" — using identity as a weapon to silence any criticism of the antisemitism within the claim.

Source: David Hirsh, Contemporary Left Antisemitism (Routledge, 2018)

Virtue Signaling — Social Currency

On social media, expressing moral positions yields social capital. A Jew who criticizes Israel may receive rewards in the form of media visibility, academic prestige, conference invitations, and publication opportunities.

The data suggests that the social reward for criticism may outweigh the social cost of disconnecting from the community.

Source: Jillian Jordan et al., "Why Do We Hate Hypocrites?", Psychological Science (2017)

✅ The Distinction — Between Legitimate Criticism and Harmful Action

This article does not call for silencing criticism. Criticism is an essential component of democracy. The analysis aims to distinguish between constructive criticism and action that harms the collective interest.

🛠 The 3D Test — Natan Sharansky

Sharansky defined 3 tests to distinguish between legitimate criticism and antisemitism:

D — Demonization: Comparing Israel to Nazis, dehumanization of Israelis.

D — Double Standards: Demands not required of any other country in the world.

D — Delegitimization: Denying Israel's right to exist as a Jewish state.

If criticism fails even one of these three tests — it has crossed the line.

Source: Natan Sharansky, "3D Test of Anti-Semitism," Jewish Political Studies Review (2004)

🔍 The Intent Test — 4 Questions

Before you judge criticism, ask:

1. Is the criticism intended to improve Israel, or to destroy it?

2. Is the critic also willing to praise when Israel does right?

3. Is the criticism directed at Israel alone, or also at other countries in similar situations?

4. Is the critic using their Jewish identity as a shield against criticism?
