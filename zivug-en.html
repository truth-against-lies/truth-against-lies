📜 What the Torah Really Says

Before we expose the barriers — let's clarify what the Torah actually teaches. The answer may surprise you: the Torah built no walls between Jews. All the walls were built by humans.

Unity of the Jewish People — One Law for All

❝All of Israel are responsible for one another❞Shevuot 39a

The most fundamental principle in Judaism: All of Israel are responsible for one another (Shevuot 39a). Not "all Ashkenazim are responsible for each other." Not "all Haredim are responsible for each other." All of Israel.

The Torah gives one system of laws for the entire people — no classes, no castes, no "first-class Jews" and "second-class Jews." At the giving of the Torah it says: "He became King in Jeshurun when the heads of the people gathered, the tribes of Israel together" (Deuteronomy 33:5) — God's kingship rests only when Israel is united.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 27b) explains that mutual responsibility means every Jew is accountable for every other Jew — not by ethnicity, not by denomination, not by skin color. One.

Sources: Shevuot 39a | Deuteronomy 33:5 | Sanhedrin 27b

Marriage Between Tribes — Permitted and Blessed

❝One law and one ordinance shall be for you and for the stranger who sojourns with you❞Numbers 15:16

A fact many don't know: There is ZERO Torah prohibition on marriage between Jews of different tribes, ethnicities, or ethnic groups. None. Zero.

Marriage between tribes was common and blessed. The Talmud (Yevamot 76a) establishes that converts are fully Jewish in every respect — including marriage. Any attempt to discriminate between Jews in matters of marriage — has no basis whatsoever in Torah.

Ruth the Moabite married Boaz — and from them came King David. This is not marginal — it's the central message: God chose that the Messiah would descend specifically from a "mixed" marriage with a convert.

Sources: Yevamot 76a | Ruth 4:13-22 | Kiddushin 68b | Numbers 15:15-16

"One law and one ordinance shall be for you and for the stranger who sojourns with you" (Numbers 15:15-16). There isn't one law for "born" Jews and another for converts. One law.

Anyone who discriminates against converts or their descendants in matchmaking — violates a Torah prohibition repeated more than any other. Maimonides (Laws of Character Traits 6:4) rules that one who causes distress to a convert violates three negative commandments.

Sources: Bava Metzia 59b | Numbers 15:15-16 | Deuteronomy 10:19 | Maimonides, Laws of Character Traits 6:4

Anyone who rejects a match because someone is a convert or descendant of converts — rejects the same spiritual DNA that created the Messiah, Rabbi Akiva, and Onkelos.

🚫 The Man-Made Barriers

Now that we've seen what the Torah says — let's see what humans have done. Every barrier separating Jews in matters of marriage is a human invention, not a divine commandment.

The Ashkenazi-Mizrahi Gap — Snobbery, Not Halacha

The separation between Ashkenazim and Mizrahim/Sephardim was never halachic. It was geographic — Jews lived in Europe or in Arab lands and simply didn't encounter each other.

When they came to Israel — instead of uniting, the gap became institutional snobbery. Transit camps (ma'abarot), separate neighborhoods, "Two Israels." Sami Smooha's research (2002) documented how the Ashkenazi community dominated institutions, media, military, and academia — while Mizrahim were systematically marginalized.

Today, when an Ashkenazi family refuses a match with a Mizrahi young woman — there is zero Torah basis for this. It's pure prejudice. Snobbery. Racism wrapped in religious language.

IDI survey: 60% of Israelis believe ethnic discrimination still exists — 78 years after the state's founding.

Sources: Shohat 1999 | Smooha 2002 | Israel Democracy Institute surveys

💔 The Shidduch Crisis

Beyond discrimination — there's a structural crisis in the matchmaking system itself. Thousands of young Israelis are stuck without a partner — not because they have nothing to offer, but because the system is broken.

The Age-Gap Theory — A Mathematical Problem

In the Haredi world, boys start shidduchim at age 22-23. Girls — at age 18-19. A gap of 3-4 years.

The Haredi population grows at roughly 4% per year. This means each year's cohort of girls is larger than the boys' cohort born 3-4 years earlier.

The result: a mathematical surplus of unmarried women. This isn't a problem of "pickiness" — it's a mathematical problem built into the system itself.

The simple solution — boys starting shidduchim earlier, or the gap narrowing — meets institutional resistance. Yeshiva heads don't want students "distracted" from their studies.
