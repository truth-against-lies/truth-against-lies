💰 Deep Investigation: Qatari Money to Hamas

$1.8 billion+ in cash suitcases — policy analysis and its consequences

The Physical Mechanism — Suitcases of Cash Through Erez

2012–2023
Qatari ambassador Mohammed al-Emadi physically entered Gaza through the Erez crossing every month, carrying suitcases full of cash. The money was transferred directly to Hamas — not through banks, not through international oversight, not through the Palestinian Authority. After 2018, Israel required that some of the funds be channeled through fuel vouchers and UN aid coupons — but Hamas continued to control distribution.
⚠ The mechanism in practice:

Israel approved the entry of cash into the Gaza Strip. Oversight was partial and insufficient, particularly regarding the salary component.

Sources: New York Times | Wall Street Journal | Times of Israel | Channel 12

Political and security echelons

⚠ The significance:

The data indicates this was a coordinated policy involving multiple elements of the security establishment, led by the Prime Minister.

Sources: Haaretz | Channel 12 | Jerusalem Post | Ynet | Times of Israel

Netanyahu's Quote — March 2019

Likud faction meeting
"Anyone who wants to thwart the establishment of a Palestinian state should support the strengthening of Hamas and the transfer of money to them. This is part of our strategy — to isolate the Palestinians in Gaza from the Palestinians in Judea and Samaria."
This quote, first published in Channel 12 and Haaretz, points to the political logic behind the money policy: preventing a Palestinian state by splitting Gaza from the West Bank. Hamas, according to this logic, was perceived as a "strategic asset."
⚠ The outcome:

The organization defined as an "asset" carried out the deadliest terror attack in Israel's history on October 7, 2023.

Sources: Channel 12 | Haaretz | Times of Israel | New York Times

Who Warned? — The Rejected Warnings

2016–2023
December 2016
Avigdor Lieberman (Defense Minister) wrote a formal memo warning that Qatari money was directly funding Hamas's military wing and that the policy would lead to a "security disaster." The memo was rejected.
2019–2020
Shin Bet reported that Mohammed Deif was diverting $4 million/month from Qatari funds to military development: offensive tunnels, rockets, drones, naval capabilities. The report did not lead to a policy change.
2019–2021
Intelligence officials speaking anonymously to Channel 13 and the Wall Street Journal said the money was "creating a monster" and that Hamas was "building an army." The political echelon dismissed it: "Better money and quiet than war."
September 2023
One month before October 7: Israel asked Qatar to increase the transfers. Hamas had already completed training for Operation "Al-Aqsa Flood."
⚠ The significance:

All warnings — from Lieberman, Shin Bet, and intelligence officials — were rejected. The policy continued until September 2023, when Israel even asked to increase the transfers.

Sources: Lieberman Memo | Shin Bet | Channel 13 | Wall Street Journal | New York Times

Official Justifications — "Quiet for Cash"

Political and security establishment arguments
Argument 1: "The money prevents a humanitarian crisis that would create international pressure on Israel"
Argument 2: "Hamas that is busy with civilian management doesn't engage in terror" — the Conception
Argument 3: "If we stop the money, Hamas will start a war"
Argument 4: "Better a strong Hamas that is responsible than chaos in Gaza"
Argument 5: "The money goes to civilians, not the military" — contradicted by Shin Bet reports
⚠ What actually happened:

The events of October 7, 2023, disproved all five assumptions. Hamas was not deterred, some funds reached the military wing, and the "quiet" period was used for military preparation.

Sources: IDF and Shin Bet internal investigations | Shin Bet reports | Haaretz | Channel 12

"The Conception" — Hamas Is Deterred, Hamas Is an Asset

The thinking failure underlying the policy
"The Conception" was the assumption that Hamas was "deterred from fighting" and preferred civilian governance over confrontation. This assumption enabled the justification of the money policy. The key figures:

• Tamir Hayman (AMAN chief): "Hamas is very deterred from war" (June 2019)
• Aharon Haliva (AMAN chief): "I expected quiet for 5 years"
• Aviv Kochavi (Chief of Staff): led the "Hamas = asset" policy
• The Cabinet: accepted intelligence assessments without challenge
⚠ What actually happened:

The deterrence assumption did not hold. Hamas used the funds to strengthen its military capabilities, and used the "quiet" period for operational preparations. The events of October 7 disproved the Conception's assumptions.

Sources: IDF internal investigations and State Comptroller reports | Channel 12 | Haaretz | Jerusalem Post

• Shin Bet warnings about diversion of funds to the military wing were rejected by the political echelon
• The mechanism created a "false mutual dependency" — Israel thought Hamas "needed" the money too much to risk it
• Hamas exploited the "quiet" as a period of strategic buildup

Sources: Shin Bet | Haaretz | Channel 12 | Times of Israel | Ynet

🏛 UNRWA — Findings and Criticism

Findings regarding the operations of the UN agency for Palestinian refugees

UNRWA Employees on October 7

January 2024 — Israeli intelligence disclosure
Israel presented intelligence that 12 UNRWA employees directly participated in the October 7 massacre. The UN investigation (OIOS) confirmed involvement of 9 employees and fired them. Additionally, according to Israeli intelligence claims, ~1,200 employees (10% of 12,000) were found to have ties to Hamas/Islamic Jihad, of whom 190 were identified terror operatives.
⚠ The outcome:

16 countries froze funding totaling $440M+. The Knesset voted 92-10 to ban UNRWA operations in Israel.

Sources: CNN | NPR | Times of Israel | Wall Street Journal | NBC News

Hamas Tunnel Under UNRWA Headquarters

February 2024 — IDF discovery
The IDF uncovered a 700-meter tunnel directly beneath UNRWA headquarters in Gaza City, with a Hamas data center and computer servers, an electrical room with industrial batteries, living quarters, a kitchen, and meeting rooms. Electrical cables ran directly from the UNRWA building to the tunnel. The walls were covered with al-Qassam Brigades posters.
⚠ The outcome:

Lazzarini (UNRWA chief): "We didn't know what was under the headquarters." IDF commander: "There is no doubt that UNRWA employees knew."

Sources: Times of Israel | NPR | Jerusalem Post

Rockets in UNRWA Schools

In the third incident, approximately 20 missiles were found. UNRWA returned the rockets to "local authorities" — i.e., to Hamas. A UN commission of inquiry determined it was "very likely" that Hamas used UNRWA schools for weapons storage and firing.
⚠ The outcome:

UNRWA discovered Hamas weapons — and returned them to Hamas.

Sources: Official UNRWA statements | Times of Israel | UN Watch
