✅ What Is the Truth?

Most ultra-Orthodox are pragmatic "non-Zionists" — not anti-Zionists

The distinction is critical: "non-Zionist" means "I don't accept Zionist ideology as a substitute for the Torah, but I live in the state, participate in elections, and receive services from it." Only a tiny fraction (less than 1%) identify with an extreme political anti-Zionist position.

④ Roots — What Happened in 1897?

The schism developed over decades, from the First Zionist Congress to the establishment of the state. Understanding the historical context is essential for analyzing the dispute.

1882

Early Immigration and the Hovevei Zion Movement

Many religious Jews were among the first to immigrate to the Land of Israel. Rabbi Shmuel Mohilever, one of the founders of Hovevei Zion, was an Orthodox rabbi who worked passionately for settling the land. Practical Zionism began from a religious impulse.

1896

Herzl Publishes "The Jewish State"

Theodor Herzl, a secular Viennese journalist, proposes a state for Jews as a solution to antisemitism. Rabbis were divided: some saw him as a messenger from Heaven, others feared that a secular state would replace the Torah.

1897

The First Zionist Congress, Basel

Herzl convenes the First Zionist Congress. Rabbi Mohilever supports it, but many rabbis oppose it. Rabbinic opposition was not one-sided — some supported political Zionism but opposed the "cultural Zionism" of Ahad Ha'am, who wanted to build a secular Jewish culture.

1902

"Altneuland" — The Vision That Divided

In his book "Altneuland" (Old New Land), Herzl describes a modern Jewish state with religious freedom, advanced technology, and equality. Orthodox rabbis saw in this vision an attempt to replace the Torah with European values. Ahad Ha'am criticized the book for not being "Jewish enough."

1912

Founding of Agudat Yisrael

European rabbis found "Agudat Yisrael" as an alternative to the Zionist movement. The goal: Orthodox Jewish representation that was non-Zionist. Importantly: they were not "against the Land of Israel" — they were against a secular state replacing the Torah as the basis for Jewish identity.

📜 Primary Source

Herzl's Declaration to the Rabbis (1897)

"Zionism will do nothing that would harm the religious sentiments of any segment of Jewry"

Herzl said this at the First Congress to reassure the rabbis. In practice, the Zionist movement was diverse — and some of its activists indeed wanted a completely secular culture. Herzl himself oscillated between the two approaches.

Protocols of the First Zionist Congress, Basel, 1897
✅ What Is the Truth?

The ultra-Orthodox fear was not imagined — but neither was it absolute

There were elements in the Zionist movement that genuinely wanted to create a "new Jew" — secular, disconnected from religion. But there were also clearly Zionist rabbis (Mohilever, Reines, Kalischer). The fear was legitimate — but the generalization that "all Zionism = destruction of religion" was inaccurate.

⑤ Herzl — The Man Behind the Myth

Herzl is one of the most controversial figures in the debate between the ultra-Orthodox and Zionists. Below is an examination of the relevant sources.

Theodor (Binyamin Ze'ev) Herzl
1860–1904 | Budapest → Vienna → Basel

Journalist, playwright, author, and political leader. The father of political Zionism. Grew up in an assimilated Jewish environment in Budapest. Until 1895, he barely engaged with Judaism. The Dreyfus Affair (1894) changed his life.

⚠ Claim Against Herzl

"Herzl wanted to convert all Jews to Christianity"

In his diary from 1893, before he became a Zionist, Herzl wrote an idea that he himself abandoned: "I will lead them [the Jews of Vienna] to St. Stephen's Cathedral on a Sunday, at noon, on the street and in broad daylight." He thought assimilation would solve antisemitism.

Herzl's Diaries, 1893 (two years before writing "The Jewish State")
↩ What Actually Happened?

Herzl completely abandoned this idea

By 1895, Herzl had undergone a transformation. After witnessing antisemitism in Paris (the Dreyfus Affair), he understood that assimilation would not work — antisemitism pursues Jews even after conversion. From that moment, he dedicated his life to the Zionist solution — a Jewish state, not assimilation.

⚠ Claim Against Herzl

"Herzl had a Christmas tree in his home"

In 1895, Herzl's wife placed a Christmas tree in their home in Vienna. Herzl wrote that he did not want his children celebrating Christian holidays, but lost the argument with his wife. This fact appears in anti-Zionist writings as "proof" of his apostasy.

↩ The Full Context

Herzl returned to Judaism — and deepened in it

In 1897, Herzl wrote the story "The Menorah" — an autobiographical tale about a secular Jew who rediscovers the beauty of the menorah and Jewish identity. In 1898, he approached the Ottoman Sultan and the German Kaiser as the representative of the Jewish people. In 1902, he wrote in "Altneuland" about a renewed Temple. His will (1904): "Transfer my remains to the Land of Israel."

⚠ Claim Against Herzl

"Herzl said: We'll lock the rabbis in their synagogues"

In his diary, Herzl wrote that in the future Jewish state, rabbis would receive great honor but would not rule in politics — "just as the army stays in its barracks." He wanted separation of religion and state, not abolition of religion.

↩ Response

Prominent rabbis supported Herzl

Rabbi Yitzchak Yaakov Reines — founded "Mizrachi" (1902), a religious-Zionist movement. He saw Zionism as a tool for saving the Jewish people, not a substitute for Torah. Rabbi Shmuel Mohilever — was among the first Zionists, even before Herzl. Rabbi Tzvi Hirsch Kalischer — published "Derishat Zion" (1862), arguing that settling the Land of Israel is a positive commandment.

1893

Conversion Idea in Diary — Immediately Abandoned

Herzl raises the idea of mass conversion. He never published it, never acted on it, and abandoned it completely within two years.

1895

The Dreyfus Affair — The Turning Point

Herzl covers the Dreyfus trial in Paris. He hears the crowd shouting "Death to the Jews!" He realizes: assimilation has failed. He begins writing "The Jewish State."

1897

"The Menorah" — Return to Roots

Herzl writes a story about a secular Jew who discovers the beauty of the Jewish candle. "The first light: one candle burns... we will rejoice in their light, we will educate our children by the light of these candles."

1904

Herzl's Death — The Will

Herzl dies at age 44, exhausted from his work. In his will, he requested: "Transfer my remains to the Land of Israel when the Jewish people establish their state." In 1949, his remains were brought to Mount Herzl in Jerusalem.

✅ What Is the Truth?

Herzl did not want to destroy Judaism. He wanted to save the Jewish people.

Yes, in 1893 he had a thought about assimilation — which he himself abandoned. His evolution is clear to anyone who reads his writings chronologically: from an assimilated journalist (1893) → state visionary (1896) → leader rediscovering his roots (1897) → a will requesting burial in the Land of Israel (1904). To claim that "Herzl wanted to destroy Judaism" is to take a single line from a diary entry in 1893 and ignore 11 years of life that followed.

⑥ The Three Oaths — The Halakhic Debate

The central claim of the halakhic anti-Zionists: "It is forbidden for Jews to ascend to the Land of Israel by force before the coming of the Messiah." The source? Three oaths that God imposed on Israel and the nations of the world.

📜 The Original Text

Babylonian Talmud, Ketubot 111a

"What are these three oaths? One — that Israel shall not ascend as a wall. One — that the Holy One, blessed be He, made Israel swear not to rebel against the nations of the world. And one — that the Holy One, blessed be He, made the nations of the world swear not to subjugate Israel excessively."
Babylonian Talmud, Ketubot 111a, expounded by Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina on Song of Songs 2:7
⚠ The Anti-Zionist Claim

"The Oaths are halakhically binding — establishing a state is forbidden"

The Satmar Rebbe (Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum) in his book "Vayoel Moshe" (1961) argued that the Oaths are binding halakhah, and that their violation by the Zionists is the cause of Israel's troubles — including the Holocaust.

Vayoel Moshe, Essay on the Three Oaths, Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum, 1961
↩ Response 1

"It is aggadah" — it is not halakhah

The majority of halakhic authorities throughout the generations did not count the Three Oaths as binding halakhah. Maimonides does not mention them in the Mishneh Torah. The Tur and the Shulchan Arukh do not cite them. The reason: the text appears as "aggadah" (homiletical exposition, not law) and as the sages established: "One does not derive halakhah from aggadah" (Jerusalem Talmud, Peah 2:4).

Jerusalem Talmud, Peah 2:4 | Maimonides did not count the Oaths among the 613 commandments
↩ Response 2

The Ramban: Settling the Land — a commandment!

The Ramban (Rabbi Moshe ben Nachman, 13th century) counts settling the Land of Israel as a positive Torah commandment (commandment number 4 in his glosses on Maimonides' Sefer HaMitzvot): "We were commanded to inherit the land... and not to abandon it to any other nation, or to desolation." If settling the Land is a commandment — the Oaths cannot nullify it.

Ramban, Glosses on Sefer HaMitzvot, Positive Commandment 4
↩ Response 3

The nations violated their oath

The third oath says: "that they shall not subjugate Israel excessively." After pogroms, expulsions, the Inquisition, and ultimately the Holocaust — which, by any measure, no "excessively" can describe what the Jewish people endured — it is clear the nations violated their oath. And if one side violates — the oath is nullified.

Maharsha, Chiddushei Aggadot, Ketubot 111; Rabbi Meir Simcha of Dvinsk, Or Sameach
↩ Response 4

The nations' approval = nullification of "ascending as a wall"

The Balfour Declaration (1917) and UN Resolution 181 (1947) constitute formal approval by the nations of the world for establishing a Jewish state. If "ascending as a wall" means "without the nations' permission" — then permission was granted. Rabbi Shlomo Zalman Auerbach also held that UN recognition nullifies the issue of the Oaths.

Balfour Declaration 1917 | UN Resolution 181, November 1947
↩ Response 5

The Zohar: "After a thousand years" — a prophecy fulfilled

The Zohar (Parashat Vayera, folio 117) states that after the exile of the children of Ishmael lasts "a thousand years," the children of Israel will return to their land. The Jews were finally exiled from their land in 636-640 CE (the Arab conquest). A thousand years later = 1636-1640. The Hovevei Zion movement began in 1840 (the Hebrew year 5600). According to the Zohar itself, the time had come.

Zohar, Parashat Vayera, folio 117a
📜 Source

Sanhedrin 32 — The Key

"Two camels ascending a narrow pass... they should not both ascend together — how is it done? The unladen one yields to the laden one"

The sages themselves use the image of "two camels on a narrow path" — when two principles conflict, one must examine which is "laden" (more important). The commandment to settle the Land (the Ramban) is "laden" more heavily than aggadah that most halakhic authorities did not accept as law.

Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 32b
✅ What Is the Truth?

The overwhelming majority of halakhic authorities do not accept the Oaths as binding law

Five solid responses: (1) Aggadah, not halakhah; (2) The Ramban commands settlement; (3) The nations violated; (4) International approval was given; (5) The Zohar itself establishes that the time had come. "Vayoel Moshe" is an extreme minority opinion — great Torah scholars such as Rabbi Kook, Rabbi Soloveitchik, Rabbi Ovadia Yosef, and the majority of halakhic authorities did not accept his position.

⑦ Key Figures in the Debate

The central figures who shaped the schism — leaders from both sides.

Rabbi Yosef Chaim Sonnenfeld
1848–1932 | Slovakia → Jerusalem

A prominent Jerusalem rabbi, one of the founders of the Edah HaCharedit. He immigrated to the Land of Israel in 1873 — out of love for the land, not out of Zionism. He opposed secular Zionism but supported ultra-Orthodox settlement in the land. His dispute was with Rabbi Kook — not over living in the land itself, but over cooperation with secular Jews.

Jacob Israel de Haan
1881–1924 | Netherlands → Jerusalem

A Dutch-Jewish jurist, journalist, and author. He immigrated to the land as a Zionist, then shifted to the ultra-Orthodox side, and served as a diplomatic representative of the Edah HaCharedit before the Arabs and the British. On June 30, 1924, he was murdered outside Sha'are Zedek Hospital in Jerusalem.

📜 Historical Testimony

Avraham Tehomi Confesses to de Haan's Murder

"Nothing was done without an order... The order came from above — from Yitzhak Ben-Zvi"

In 1985, in a television interview, Avraham Tehomi (a member of the Haganah organization) admitted he carried out the murder under direct orders. Yitzhak Ben-Zvi later became Israel's second president. This is a dark stain on Zionist history that must be acknowledged.

Television interview, Avraham Tehomi, 1985; Shalom Cohen, "De Haan: The First Political Murder in the Land of Israel"
The Satmar Rebbe — Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum
1887–1979 | Satu Mare (Romania) → USA

Author of "Vayoel Moshe" (1961), the central book against Zionism from a halakhic perspective. He survived the Holocaust on Zionist funds (the Kasztner train) — a fact he himself found difficult to explain. He claimed the Holocaust was punishment for Zionism. He built a massive community in Williamsburg.

⚠ Central Claim of Vayoel Moshe

"The Holocaust was divine punishment for Zionism"

In "Vayoel Moshe," the Satmar Rebbe argues that the Zionists "violated the Oaths" and thereby caused divine wrath that manifested in the Holocaust. He cites sources from the Zohar and Midrashim to support this claim.

↩ The Lubavitcher Rebbe's Response

"Any attempt to explain the Holocaust as punishment — is reprehensible"

The Chabad Rebbe (Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson) vehemently opposed this claim: "No one can say the Holocaust was punishment. Whoever says so insults the memory of six million — including millions of innocent children, people of faith, righteous and holy people." The Rebbe added: "The Holocaust is a destruction that has no human explanation."

Talks of the Lubavitcher Rebbe, 16 Tammuz 5738 (1978)
Neturei Karta — The Controversial Group
Founded 1938 | Jerusalem

A small group (a few hundred families) that split from the Edah HaCharedit. They became known primarily for participating in Ahmadinejad's Holocaust denial conference in Tehran (2006) and in pro-Palestinian demonstrations. They are the most visible face of ultra-Orthodox anti-Zionism — but represent less than 0.2% of the ultra-Orthodox.

✅ What Is the Truth About Neturei Karta?

Their theology is rooted — their politics are reprehensible

They have a halakhic basis (the Three Oaths), and their principled opposition to Zionism shares roots with the Edah HaCharedit's position. However: participation in a Holocaust denial conference, embracing Ahmadinejad, and appearing alongside Hamas and Hezbollah — are completely reprehensible. Satmar itself condemns them. The Satmar Rebbe (Rabbi Aharon) declared that Neturei Karta "went beyond the pale" and forbade his followers from any contact with them.

⑧ Religious Zionism Responds

Facing ultra-Orthodox opposition, a rabbinic position crystallized that sees Zionism as part of the process of redemption. Below are the central arguments.

Rabbi Abraham Isaac HaKohen Kook
1865–1935 | Latvia → Land of Israel | First Chief Rabbi

Rabbi Kook saw Zionism as "atchalta de'geulah" — the beginning of redemption. According to his approach, even the secular pioneers who built the land were "unconscious agents" of divine providence. "Their inner soul is holy, even if they themselves don't know it."

📜 Famous Quote

Rabbi Kook — Orot HaTeshuvah

"The old shall be renewed, and the new shall be sanctified"

Eight words that summarize Rabbi Kook's entire philosophy: the old Torah will receive new faces, and the new state will become sanctified over time. There is no contradiction between old and new — there is partnership.

Rabbi Kook, Igrot HaRe'iyah
Rabbi Joseph Dov Soloveitchik
1903–1993 | Poland → USA | "The Rav" of America

The greatest halakhic authority of Modern Orthodox Judaism in America. In his sermon "Kol Dodi Dofek" (1956), he described six "knockings" — six ways in which God "knocks on the door" through the establishment of the State of Israel.

📜 The Six Knockings

Kol Dodi Dofek (1956)

Knock 1: The military miracle — a small army defeated the Arab armies.
Knock 2: International recognition — the UN recognized the Jewish state.
Knock 3: Theological — Christianity and Islam were forced to recognize a sovereign Jewish reality.
Knock 4: "Captive children" returning — secular Jews connect to their roots through the state.
Knock 5: The state as refuge — saving Jews from Islamic countries, from Ethiopia, from the Soviet Union.
Knock 6: Jewish blood has value — for the first time in history, there is someone who fights for Jewish blood.

Rabbi Joseph Dov Soloveitchik, Kol Dodi Dofek, 1956
Rabbi Yitzchak Yaakov Reines
1839–1915 | Founder of the "Mizrachi" movement

Founded the "Mizrachi" ("Merkaz Ruchani" / Spiritual Center) movement in 1902 — a religious-Zionist movement. His argument: "Zionism is not a substitute for Torah, but a political tool for saving the physical lives of Israel. It is permitted and commanded to use this tool."

⚠ Ultra-Orthodox Claim

"Secular people cannot build redemption — only the Messiah can"

The claim: Redemption must come from above, through the Messiah son of David, and not through secular pioneers draining swamps. A state built by heretics cannot be part of the divine plan.

↩ Response

Cyrus the Persian built the Second Temple — and wasn't even Jewish

After the destruction of the First Temple, who gave the order to rebuild? Cyrus, King of Persia — a gentile. And the prophet Isaiah calls him "my anointed" (Isaiah 45:1): "Thus says the Lord to his anointed, to Cyrus." If God can use a gentile king to build a Temple — He can use secular pioneers to build a state.

Isaiah 45:1 | Ezra 1:1-4
📜 The Prayer for the State

"The beginning of the flowering of our redemption"

"Our Father in Heaven, Rock of Israel and its Redeemer, bless the State of Israel, the beginning of the flowering of our redemption"

The phrasing "the beginning of the flowering" is very precise: not "the redemption," but "the beginning of the flowering of our redemption." It is a process, not a state. The state is not the redemption — it is the first step.

Formulated by Chief Rabbi Herzog, Chief Rabbinate of Israel, 1948
✅ What Is the Truth?

Religious Zionism did not "invent" sources — it brought forth sources that were always there

The Ramban (13th century), Rabbi Tzvi Hirsch Kalischer (1862), Rabbi Shmuel Mohilever (1882) — all preceded Herzl by decades and centuries and supported settling the Land. Religious Zionism did not invent a new Torah. It recalled an old Torah.

⑨ What Zionism Got Right — and What It Got Wrong

A balanced analysis requires acknowledging both sides. Zionism saved lives, built a state, and gathered exiles. At the same time, there are also documented cases of harm to religious communities. Both sides are documented in the sources.

⚠ What Went Wrong

The Yemenite Children Affair

In the 1950s, hundreds (and possibly thousands) of children of immigrants from Yemen, North Africa, and the Balkans were taken from their parents. Parents were sent home with the message that the baby had died — without a body, without a grave. Three investigative commissions failed to provide complete answers. This wound is still open.

The Sidelocks Decree and the Melting Pot

Children of Yemenite and Moroccan immigrants were forced to cut their sidelocks. Children of Eastern European immigrants underwent "secularization" in transit camps. The "melting pot" policy demanded that all immigrants abandon their cultural identity and become a "new Israeli" — secular, Ashkenazi, working the land.

The Altalena Affair (1948)

On June 22, 1948, on Ben-Gurion's orders, the IDF fired on the Irgun's ship "Altalena" off the coast of Tel Aviv. 16 Jews were killed by Jews. Menachem Begin, commander of the Irgun, prevented civil war — but the cost was heavy.

Marginalization of the Sacred

In the early years of the state, MAPAI (Ben-Gurion's party) members saw religious culture as "diasporic" and the religious as "outdated." Shabbat radio broadcasts, insensitivity to kashrut in the army, and contempt for Mizrachi culture — all deeply wounded religious Jews.

✨ What Went Right

Shabbat as an Official Day of Rest

Israel is the only country in the world where Shabbat is the official day of rest. Public transportation stops, many stores close, the memorial day siren — all of these result from Zionism incorporating Jewish elements into the national identity.

Kashrut and Personal Status Laws

Kashrut in the IDF, the Rabbinical Courts, the Law of Return ("every Jew has the right to immigrate") — these are not taken for granted. They are the result of agreements between religious and secular Zionism (status quo) that protect the Jewish character of the state.

Institutionalized and Funded Religious Education

The state funds yeshivot, religious schools, and ultra-Orthodox communities. No other country in the world funds Torah study on this scale. This is a privilege that did not exist in any exile.

Ingathering of Exiles — Saving Lives

Operation Magic Carpet (Yemen), Operation Ezra and Nehemiah (Iraq), Operations Moses and Solomon (Ethiopia), immigration from the Soviet Union — the Zionist state saved millions of Jews from real danger to their lives. Without a state — where would they have been?

✅ What Is the Truth?

Both the harms and the achievements — are real. Both must be acknowledged.

Those who say "Zionism was only good" ignore the Yemenite children, the melting pot, and the harm to traditional Jews. Those who say "Zionism was only bad" ignore the saving of millions, Shabbat as a day of rest, and the yeshivot that study thanks to the state. The truth, as usual, is complex.

⑩ A View from the Torah, Zohar, and Kabbalah

Beyond the halakhic and political debate, Kabbalistic sources, the Zohar, and Hasidic thought offer an additional perspective on the schism and on unity.

📜 Zohar — Parashat Vayera

The Prophecy of the Year 5600 (1840)

"In the six hundredth year of the sixth millennium, the gates of wisdom above and the wellsprings of wisdom below will be opened"

"In the six hundredth year of the sixth millennium [= year 5600, 1840], the gates of wisdom above and the wellsprings of wisdom below will be opened." The Zohar prophesied that in 1840 two processes would begin: a spiritual awakening (gates of wisdom above) and material progress (wellsprings of wisdom below). And indeed, at precisely that time, both the Hovevei Zion movement and the Industrial Revolution began.

Zohar, Parashat Vayera, folio 117a

Sparks of Holiness — The Arizal

"In every place, there are holy sparks that need to be refined and elevated"

According to the teachings of the Arizal (Rabbi Isaac Luria, 16th century), holy sparks fell to every place in the world, and every Jew must "refine" and "elevate" them. According to the religious-Zionist interpretation: the pioneers who built the land — even if they were secular — were refining sparks. They turned desolation into a garden, thereby elevating the holiness embedded in the soil.

It'aruta D'l'tata — Awakening from Below

In Kabbalah, there are two types of awakening: it'aruta d'l'eila (from above, from God) and it'aruta d'l'tata (from below, from man). Religious Zionism argues: if we wait only for "from above" without acting "from below" — we are ignoring half of the Torah. The Zohar states: "The awakening from below arouses the awakening from above" — human action invites divine blessing.

📜 Berakhot 58

"Just as their faces are different from one another..."

"Just as their faces are different from one another, so too their opinions are different from one another"

The sages teach that the diversity of opinions is divine design, not an accident. The differences between ultra-Orthodox and Zionists, between Ashkenazim and Mizrachim, between secular and religious — are part of the plan. The Torah does not want uniformity. It wants unity within diversity.

Babylonian Talmud, Berakhot 58a
📜 Vayikra Rabbah

The Four Species = Four Types of Jews

Etrog (taste and fragrance) = a Jew with Torah and good deeds.
Lulav (taste without fragrance) = Torah without good deeds.
Myrtle (fragrance without taste) = good deeds without Torah.
Willow (no taste nor fragrance) = neither Torah nor good deeds.
What does God do? "Bind them all together as one bundle and they will atone for each other." All together — not just the righteous.

Vayikra Rabbah 30:12

"Captive Children" — Maimonides and the Chazon Ish

Maimonides (Laws of Rebels 3:3) rules that a Jew who grew up without Jewish education is a "captive child" — like a child who was captured by gentiles and raised without knowing he was Jewish. Not guilty. The Chazon Ish (Rabbi Avraham Yeshaya Karelitz) extended this to our generation: most secular Jews today are captive children — not heretics, not rebels, but people who never received authentic Jewish education. Our obligation is to draw them closer, not push them away.

"Pintele Yid" — Chabad

In Chabad Hasidism, every Jew — even the most distant — has a "pintele Yid" (a Jewish spark) that cannot be extinguished. This is the divine soul found in every Jew without exception. The Lubavitcher Rebbe sent emissaries to every corner of the world not to judge Jews — but to awaken the inner spark within them.

✅ What Is the Truth?

The Torah itself teaches that unity must include everyone — even the "willow"

From the Zohar to the Chazon Ish, from the Arizal to the Lubavitcher Rebbe, the inner language of Judaism says the same thing: diversity is not fracture, it is design. And the "bundle" — the binding together of all four species — is the goal.

⑪ The Path to Unity

💌 A Message to the Secular Jew

The very fact that you have Jewish roots is already a sign that your family's past was close to Torah. Your grandmother lit Shabbat candles. Your grandfather said Kaddish. They were not "primitive" — they preserved a chain of 3,000 years that reached you.

You do not have to be religious. But respect your history. Know it before you judge it. Know where you came from before you decide where you are going.

And the ultra-Orthodox person you see on the street? He is not a "parasite" or a "fanatic" — he is preserving a tradition that kept the Jewish people alive for 2,000 years. Without him, you would not exist today. Think about that.

📜 A Message to the Ultra-Orthodox Jew

The secular person operates more with logic and less with faith. That is not "heresy" — it is the way he understands the world. The very fact that you have not succeeded in conveying Torah and Judaism to him through healthy logic — is your failure. Not his.

The Torah says "Know what to answer" (Pirkei Avot 2:14). If you cannot explain Judaism in a language that a thinking person understands — the problem is with your explanation, not with his intellect.

And the secular person who fights in the IDF? He is risking his life so that you can study Torah in safety. Without the state he defends — where would you be studying? Maimonides says (Laws of Torah Study 3:10): "All Torah without work — will ultimately cease and lead to sin."

📜 The Summary

Two Answers — From the Same Love

When antisemitism, pogroms, and the Holocaust said "You have no place in the world" — the Jewish people answered with two voices:

Zionism answered: "Act. Build. Create. Take destiny into your own hands."

Ultra-Orthodox Judaism answered: "Keep Torah. Wait for redemption. Let God lead."

Both answers stem from the same love of Israel. The gap between them is about how — not about why. Both say: "The Jewish people must continue to live." The argument is about the path, not the destination.
