Be Fruitful and Multiply — The First Commandment

❝And God blessed them, and God said to them: Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth❞Genesis 1:28

"Be fruitful and multiply" is the first commandment in the Torah — given to Adam and Eve before any other command. It is not merely a blessing but a binding halachic obligation on every Jewish man.

⚖ Israel's Family Court System

A dual court system, a tender years doctrine still on the books, biased social workers, parental alienation up 2,000% — here is what the system doesn't want you to know.

Dual Court System — Race to Jurisdiction

Israel has a unique and problematic system: two courts handle family matters in parallel:

The Family Court (est. 1995) — civil law.
The Rabbinical Court — exclusive jurisdiction over Jewish marriage and divorce.

Both courts can handle custody, child support, and property division. This creates the "race to jurisdiction": whoever files first chooses the arena.

Practical example: A father files in the Rabbinical Court (which applies Halacha — absolute child support obligation on the father). The mother files in Family Court (which applies the 2017 ruling — proportional sharing based on income). The outcomes can be completely different — same couple, same children, different amounts.

Trend reversal: Historically, men rushed to Rabbinical Court and women to Family Court. After the 2017 ruling reduced child support in civil courts — the trend reversed: women began filing in Rabbinical Courts, where support amounts are higher.

2025: The Supreme Court reaffirmed that Rabbinical Courts lack jurisdiction to adjudicate child support without both parties' consent.

Tender Years Doctrine — Still Law in 2026

Children under 6 go automatically to the mother. This is still law in Israel, while most Western countries abolished it decades ago.

Section 25 of the Capacity and Guardianship Law (1962) states: absent parental agreement, custody of children under 6 is presumed to belong to the mother, unless "circumstances justify deviation."

In practice: a father challenging this must prove the mother is violent, a drug abuser, or severely negligent.

Schnitt Committee (2005-2011): Recommended abolishing the presumption entirely, replacing it with "best interests of the specific child." The Justice Minister adopted the recommendations in 2011.

What actually happened? The presumption was never abolished by legislation. A bill was introduced to the Knesset but was defeated. The Rackman Center and women's organizations opposed it.

💰 Child Support — Gender-Based Discrimination

Israel is one of the only democracies where child support obligation is explicitly gender-based rather than income-based. A father pays 100% even if the mother earns more.

The Halachic Law — Father Pays, Mother Is Exempt

Israel's child support law is based on Jewish religious law (Halacha):

Ages 0-6 (essential needs): The father bears absolute obligation. He pays 100% — even if he has no means, even if the mother earns more. The mother — no obligation at all.

Amounts: NIS 1,400-2,000 per month per child (basic needs) + housing (33-50% of rent costs) + extraordinary expenses (daycare, activities, medical).

Numerical example: Father earns NIS 12,000, Mother earns NIS 15,000 — two children ages 3 and 5:
Basic needs: NIS 1,400 × 2 = NIS 2,800
Housing (40% of NIS 4,000 rent): NIS 1,600
Total: ~NIS 4,400/month — 37% of the father's salary
This is before daycare (NIS 2,000-3,000 per child), activities, and extraordinary expenses. The mother, who earns more — pays nothing.

No codified formula: Israel is among the only democracies with no official child support formula. Different judges — different amounts. People in identical situations pay wildly different amounts. Minimum self-support reserve: ~NIS 5,500.
