The Enlightenment Era — 1807-1880

1807ZionismNapoleon's Sanhedrin▼

Napoleon convened 71 Jewish leaders in Paris as a "Grand Sanhedrin" to regulate the status of Jews in the French Empire. Discussions covered civic loyalty, intermarriage, and state law. A precedent for Jewish emancipation in Europe.

Source: Encyclopedia Judaica, "Napoleon and the Jews"
1840AntisemitismThe Damascus Affair▼

A Capuchin friar and his servant disappeared in the Jewish quarter of Damascus. Jews were accused of "ritual murder" and tortured until they "confessed." Moses Montefiore and Adolphe Crémieux secured the release of the prisoners. The event accelerated the founding of Alliance Israélite Universelle.

Source: Jonathan Frankel, The Damascus Affair (1997)
1858AntisemitismThe Mortara Affair▼

Edgardo Mortara, a 6-year-old Jewish boy from Bologna, was seized on papal orders after a Christian servant secretly baptized him. His family never recovered him. The affair shocked Europe and strengthened calls for an international Jewish organization.

Source: David Kertzer, The Kidnapping of Edgardo Mortara (1997)
1860AchievementsAlliance Israélite Universelle Founded▼

The first international Jewish organization was founded in Paris. "All Israel are responsible for one another" — its mission was to defend Jewish rights worldwide and establish a network of schools across the Middle East and North Africa.

Source: André Kaspi, Histoire de l'Alliance Israélite Universelle
1862ZionismMoses Hess — "Rome and Jerusalem"▼

Socialist philosopher Moses Hess published "Rome and Jerusalem" — one of the first proto-Zionist books. Hess argued that emancipation would not solve the Jewish question and that national revival in the Land of Israel was necessary.

Source: Shlomo Avineri, Moses Hess: Prophet of Communism and Zionism
1870ZionismMikveh Israel — First Agricultural School▼

The "Mikveh Israel" agricultural school was established near Jaffa by Alliance Israélite Universelle. It was the first modern Jewish educational institution in the Land of Israel, training generations of pioneers in agriculture.

Source: Jewish Virtual Library
1878ZionismFounding of Petah Tikva▼

Petah Tikva, the "Mother of Colonies," was founded as the first Jewish agricultural settlement in the Land of Israel. The settlers, from Jerusalem and Hungary, battled malaria and harsh conditions but laid the foundations for modern Jewish settlement.

Source: Encyclopedia Judaica, "Petah Tikvah"

Early Zionism — 1881-1917

1881AntisemitismRussian Pogroms Begin▼

A wave of violent pogroms erupted in southern Russia after the assassination of Tsar Alexander II. Jews were beaten, murdered, and their property looted. The pogroms continued for years and triggered a massive emigration — approximately 2 million Jews left Russia by 1914.

Source: John Klier, Russians, Jews, and the Pogroms of 1881-1882
1882ZionismThe First Aliyah▼

Approximately 25,000-35,000 Jews immigrated to the Land of Israel, mainly from Russia and Romania. They founded settlements like Rishon LeZion, Zikhron Ya'akov, and Rehovot. The Hovevei Zion and BILU movements led the wave. Baron Rothschild provided financial support.

Source: Encyclopedia Judaica, "Aliyah"
1894AntisemitismThe Dreyfus Affair▼

Captain Alfred Dreyfus, a French-Jewish officer, was falsely accused of espionage for Germany. Convicted in a rigged court-martial and exiled to Devil's Island. The affair exposed deep antisemitism in France and inspired Herzl to write "The Jewish State."

Source: Britannica, "Dreyfus Affair" | Ruth Harris, Dreyfus: Politics, Emotion, and the Scandal of the Century
1896ZionismHerzl — "The Jewish State"▼

Theodor Herzl, an Austro-Hungarian journalist, published "Der Judenstaat" (The Jewish State). The book argued that the only solution to antisemitism was establishing a sovereign Jewish state. It became the founding text of the political Zionist movement.

Source: Theodor Herzl, Der Judenstaat (1896)
1897ZionismFirst Zionist Congress▼

208 delegates from 17 countries convened in Basel, Switzerland. The Congress established the World Zionist Organization and adopted the "Basel Program" — creating a national home for the Jewish people in the Land of Israel. Herzl wrote in his diary: "In Basel I founded the Jewish State."

Source: Central Zionist Archives | Michael Heymann, The Minutes of the Zionist General Council
1903AntisemitismThe Kishinev Pogrom▼

A violent pogrom erupted in Kishinev (modern-day Moldova) following a blood libel. 49 Jews were murdered, hundreds wounded, and 1,500 homes damaged. Bialik wrote "In the City of Slaughter." The event shook the Jewish world and strengthened the Zionist movement.

49 killed, hundreds wounded

Source: Steven Zipperstein, Pogrom: Kishinev and the Tilt of History (2018)
1903AntisemitismPublication of "The Protocols of the Elders of Zion"▼

The most infamous antisemitic forgery in history was published in Russia. It claimed a "Jewish plan for world domination." Proven to be a fabrication by the Russian secret police as early as 1921 by The Times, yet continues to spread hatred to this day.

Source: Norman Cohn, Warrant for Genocide (1967) | The Times, 1921
1904ZionismThe Second Aliyah▼

Approximately 35,000-40,000 Jews immigrated to the Land of Israel (1904-1914), mainly young socialists from Russia. They established the kibbutz movement, labor organizations, and the social infrastructure of the Yishuv. David Ben-Gurion arrived in this wave.

Source: Encyclopedia Judaica, "Second Aliyah"
1909AchievementsFounding of Tel Aviv and Degania▼

66 Jewish families drew lots for land plots on the sand dunes north of Jaffa — thus Tel Aviv was born, the first Hebrew city. Around the same time, the first kibbutz — Degania — was established on the shores of the Sea of Galilee, laying the foundations for the kibbutz idea.

Source: Maoz Azaryahu, Tel Aviv: Mythography of a City
1913AntisemitismThe Beilis Trial▼

Menahem Mendel Beilis, a Jew from Kiev, was accused of murdering a Christian child for "blood use" — a classic blood libel. After a sensational international trial, Beilis was acquitted, but the trial exposed the depth of antisemitism in Tsarist Russia.

Source: Edmund Levin, A Child of Christian Blood (2014)
Nov 2, 1917ZionismThe Balfour Declaration▼

British Foreign Secretary Arthur Balfour sent a letter to Lord Rothschild on behalf of the British government: "His Majesty's Government view with favour the establishment in Palestine of a national home for the Jewish people." The declaration became the first international legal basis for Jewish rights in the Land of Israel.

Source: Jonathan Schneer, The Balfour Declaration (2010)

The Mandate Period — 1918-1939

1920PeaceSan Remo Conference▼

The Allied Powers (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) ratified the Balfour Declaration and the British Mandate over Palestine. The decision granted international legal validity to the establishment of a Jewish national home.

Source: League of Nations Official Journal | San Remo Resolution, April 25, 1920
1920WarsBattle of Tel Hai▼

Hundreds of Arabs attacked the Tel Hai settlement in the Upper Galilee. Joseph Trumpeldor and 7 other defenders fell. According to tradition, Trumpeldor's last words were: "It is good to die for our country." Tel Hai became a symbol of Jewish self-defense.

8 killed

Source: Yigal Lossin, Pillar of Fire
1920AchievementsFounding of the Haganah▼

The Haganah defense organization was established as a clandestine Jewish militia to protect settlements from Arab attacks. Operating under the noses of the British, it later became the foundation of the IDF.

Source: Encyclopedia Judaica, "Haganah"
1925AchievementsHebrew University Opens▼

The Hebrew University opened in Jerusalem in an official ceremony on Mount Scopus, attended by Balfour, Allenby, and Albert Einstein. The first academic institution in Hebrew, it became one of the leading universities in the Middle East.

Source: Hebrew University Archives
1929AntisemitismHebron Massacre — 1929 Riots▼

The 1929 riots erupted across Palestine. In Hebron, 67 Jews were murdered by local Arabs, including women and children. The historic Jewish community in Hebron was completely evacuated. In Safed, Jerusalem, and Motza, Jews also fell. A total of 133 killed.

133 killed, 339 wounded

Source: Tom Segev, One Palestine, Complete (2000)
1936-1939WarsThe Arab Revolt▼

Three years of Arab violence against the Jewish Yishuv and the British. Included strikes, demonstrations, and attacks on Jewish settlements and infrastructure. The British suppressed the revolt by force. The Peel Commission (1937) recommended partition of the land for the first time.

Source: Matthew Hughes, Britain's Pacification of Palestine (2019)
1937PeacePeel Commission Report▼

The British Peel Commission recommended for the first time the partition of Palestine into a Jewish state and an Arab state. The Arabs rejected the plan entirely. The Jewish leadership debated but ultimately accepted the principle of partition.

Source: Palestine Royal Commission Report (Peel Report), July 1937
1939AntisemitismThe White Paper▼

The British government issued the "White Paper" which drastically limited Jewish immigration to Palestine to only 75,000 over five years, and banned Jewish land purchase in most of the country. At the worst possible timing — on the eve of the Holocaust — Britain closed the gates of the Land of Israel.

Source: British White Paper of 1939 | Michael Cohen, Palestine: Retreat from the Mandate

The Holocaust — 1933-1945

Jan 30, 1933HolocaustHitler Rises to Power▼

Adolf Hitler was appointed Chancellor of Germany. Within months he established totalitarian rule. On April 1, 1933 — a government boycott of Jewish businesses. The beginning of a systematic process of exclusion, persecution, and extermination of European Jewry.

Source: USHMM | Ian Kershaw, Hitler (1998)
Sep 15, 1935HolocaustNuremberg Laws▼

The Reichstag passed the Nuremberg Laws: the "Reich Citizenship Law" and the "Law for the Protection of German Blood and Honor." Jews were stripped of citizenship, intermarriage was banned, and "Jewishness" was defined on a racial basis — even those with one Jewish grandparent.

Source: USHMM, "Nuremberg Laws" | Yad Vashem
1938HolocaustÉvian Conference — The World Turns Away▼

32 nations convened in Évian, France, to discuss Jewish refugees from Germany. Nearly every country refused to accept refugees. Only the Dominican Republic offered asylum. The world knew — and did nothing.

Source: USHMM | S.S. Friedman, No Haven for the Oppressed (1973)
Nov 9-10, 1938HolocaustKristallnacht — Night of Broken Glass▼

A state-organized pogrom across Germany and Austria. 1,400 synagogues burned, 7,500 Jewish businesses destroyed, 91 Jews murdered, and 30,000 sent to concentration camps. The turning point from persecution to open violence.

91 killed, 30,000 arrested

Source: USHMM | Yad Vashem, "Kristallnacht"
1939HolocaustSS St. Louis▼

937 Jewish refugees sailed from Hamburg to Cuba aboard the SS St. Louis. Cuba, the US, and Canada all refused entry. The ship returned to Europe. Approximately 254 of the passengers were murdered in the Holocaust. A symbol of the Western world's abandonment.

~254 murdered in the Holocaust

Source: USHMM, "Voyage of the St. Louis"
Sep 29-30, 1941HolocaustBabi Yar Massacre▼

Einsatzgruppen units and Ukrainians carried out the largest mass shooting. 33,771 Kyiv Jews were shot in two days at the Babi Yar ravine. By war's end, approximately 100,000-150,000 people were murdered there.

33,771 killed in two days

Source: Yad Vashem | USHMM, "Babi Yar"
Jun 1-2, 1941AntisemitismThe Farhud — Baghdad Pogrom▼

A violent pogrom in Baghdad against the ancient Jewish community. Shavuot celebrations turned into massacre: 180 Jews murdered, hundreds wounded, and extensive property looted. The Farhud accelerated the mass emigration of Iraqi Jews to Israel.

180 killed, 1,000 wounded

Source: Shmuel Moreh, The Farhud | Yad Vashem
Jan 20, 1942HolocaustWannsee Conference — "The Final Solution"▼

15 senior Nazi Party and German government officials convened at a villa in the Wannsee suburb of Berlin. In 90 minutes they coordinated the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question" — a systematic plan to exterminate 11 million European Jews.

Source: Yad Vashem, "Wannsee Conference" | Mark Roseman, The Wannsee Conference and the Final Solution
Apr 19, 1943HolocaustWarsaw Ghetto Uprising▼

Approximately 750 Jewish fighters, led by Mordechai Anielewicz, rose in armed revolt against the Nazis as they came to liquidate the ghetto. The fighting lasted 27 days — a civilian uprising that lasted longer than the fall of entire nations. The uprising became a symbol of Jewish heroism.

Source: Yad Vashem | Israel Gutman, Resistance: The Warsaw Ghetto Uprising
Jan 27, 1945HolocaustLiberation of Auschwitz▼

The Red Army liberated the Auschwitz-Birkenau extermination camp. Soviet soldiers found approximately 7,000 emaciated survivors. About 1.1 million people were murdered in the camp, of whom approximately one million were Jews. January 27 is International Holocaust Remembrance Day.

~1.1 million murdered at Auschwitz

Source: USHMM | Yad Vashem | Auschwitz-Birkenau Memorial
1945-1946HolocaustNuremberg Trials▼

The International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg tried 24 senior Nazi leaders for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and genocide. 12 were sentenced to death. The trial laid the foundations of international criminal law.

Source: Nuremberg Trial Proceedings | USHMM

Establishment of the State — 1947-1949

Jul 4, 1946AntisemitismKielce Pogrom▼

A pogrom against Holocaust survivors in Kielce, Poland — one year after the war ended. 42 Jews who returned home were murdered by a Polish mob following a blood libel. The mob that remained hadn't changed. The pogrom triggered a massive Jewish emigration from Poland.

42 killed

Source: USHMM, "Kielce Pogrom" | Jan Gross, Fear (2006)
1947ZionismExodus 1947▼

A ship carrying 4,515 Holocaust survivors attempted to reach the Land of Israel. The British intercepted the ship, beat the passengers, and deported them back to Germany — the land of their murderers. The affair shocked world public opinion and contributed to support for the partition of Palestine.

Source: USHMM, "Exodus 1947" | Ruth Gruber, Exodus 1947
Nov 29, 1947PeaceResolution 181 — The Partition Plan▼

The UN General Assembly voted in favor of partitioning Palestine into a Jewish state and an Arab state. 33 in favor, 13 against, 10 abstentions. The Jews accepted the resolution; the Arabs rejected it. People danced in the streets of Tel Aviv; the next day Arab violence erupted.

Source: UN General Assembly Resolution 181 | Benny Morris, 1948
May 14, 1948AchievementsDeclaration of Independence▼

David Ben-Gurion declared the establishment of the State of Israel at a ceremony on Rothschild Boulevard, Tel Aviv. "We hereby declare the establishment of a Jewish state in the Land of Israel — the State of Israel." For the first time in 2,000 years — Jewish sovereignty in the Land of Israel.

Source: Israel State Archives | Declaration of Independence, 5 Iyar 5708
May 15, 1948WarsWar of Independence▼

The day after the declaration, 5 Arab armies (Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, Lebanon) invaded Israel. The war lasted 15 months. Israel, with a defense force of 30,000 fighters, defeated armies of nations with millions of citizens. 6,373 Israelis fell — 1% of the population.

6,373 Israeli casualties (1% of population)

Source: Benny Morris, 1948 (2008) | IDF Archives
May 26, 1948AchievementsEstablishment of the IDF▼

The Israel Defense Forces was officially established, unifying the Haganah, Irgun, and Lehi under a single command. Ben-Gurion signed the "IDF Order" establishing that there would be no more private militias — only one army for one state.

Source: IDF Archives | Israel State Archives
1949-1950ZionismOperation Magic Carpet — Yemenite Jews▼

Approximately 49,000 Yemenite Jews were airlifted to Israel. Most had never seen an airplane; some believed the prophecy "I bore you on eagles' wings" was being fulfilled. A community of 2,500 years immigrated almost in its entirety.

Source: Tudor Parfitt, The Road to Redemption | Israel State Archives
May 11, 1949AchievementsIsrael Admitted to the UN▼

Israel was admitted as the 59th member of the United Nations. The vote: 37 in favor, 12 against, 9 abstentions. Formal international recognition of the Jewish people's right to a sovereign state.

Source: UN General Assembly Resolution 273

Nation Building — 1950-1966

Jul 5, 1950AchievementsLaw of Return▼

The Knesset enacted the Law of Return — "Every Jew has the right to immigrate to Israel." The law enshrines the right of every Jew in the world to obtain Israeli citizenship. A legal expression of the idea that Israel is the national home of the Jewish people.

Source: Israel Knesset Archives, Law of Return 5710-1950
1950-1951ZionismOperation Ezra & Nehemiah — Iraqi Jews▼

Approximately 120,000 Iraqi Jews were airlifted to Israel after the Iraqi government stripped their citizenship and confiscated their property. A community of 2,600 years — one of the oldest in the world — was almost completely uprooted.

Source: Moshe Gat, The Jewish Exodus from Iraq (1997)
1952PeaceLuxembourg Agreement — German Reparations▼

Israel and West Germany signed a reparations agreement. Germany paid 3 billion marks to Israel as compensation for Jewish property looted during the Holocaust. The agreement was bitterly controversial in Israel — many saw the money as "blood money."

Source: Nana Sagi, German Reparations (1986)
1953AchievementsYad Vashem Established▼

The national Holocaust memorial was established in Jerusalem. Yad Vashem documents the names of 6 million victims, preserves testimonies, and educates generations. Its name comes from Isaiah 56: "I will give them in My house and within My walls a memorial and a name."

Source: Yad Vashem Official | Knesset Law 5713-1953
1956WarsSinai Campaign — Suez Crisis▼

Israel, coordinating with Britain and France, captured the Sinai Peninsula and Gaza Strip in response to Egypt's blockade of the Straits of Tiran and fedayeen attacks. Israel withdrew under American-Soviet pressure but secured freedom of navigation through the strait.

Source: Motti Golani, Israel in Search of a War (1998)
1961AchievementsEichmann Trial▼

Adolf Eichmann, responsible for the logistics of the "Final Solution," was captured by the Mossad in Argentina in 1960 and put on trial in Jerusalem. The trial was televised and for the first time the world heard Holocaust survivors' testimonies. Convicted and hanged in 1962.

Source: Deborah Lipstadt, The Eichmann Trial (2011) | Yad Vashem
1965AchievementsInvention of Drip Irrigation▼

Simcha Blass, an Israeli water engineer, developed the drip irrigation method at Kibbutz Hatzerim. Netafim, the company he founded, revolutionized world agriculture — saving 30-50% of water. Israeli technology that feeds hundreds of millions of people.

Source: Netafim History | Seth Siegel, Let There Be Water (2015)

Era of Wars — 1967-1982

Jun 5-10, 1967WarsSix-Day War▼

Israel attacked in self-defense after Egypt blockaded the Straits of Tiran, expelled UN forces from Sinai, and massed troops on the border. In 6 days Israel captured the Sinai, Golan Heights, Judea and Samaria, and the Gaza Strip. Jerusalem was reunified. The paratroopers reached the Western Wall.

Source: Michael Oren, Six Days of War (2002)
Jun 7, 1967AchievementsReunification of Jerusalem▼

"The Temple Mount is in our hands!" — Motta Gur, paratroop brigade commander. For the first time in 19 years, Jews could pray at the Western Wall. Jordan destroyed 58 synagogues in the Old City between 1948-1967. Israel maintained freedom of worship for all faiths.

Source: Abraham Rabinovich, The Battle for Jerusalem
Sep 5, 1972AntisemitismMunich Massacre▼

Terrorists from "Black September" infiltrated the Olympic Village in Munich, murdered 2 Israeli athletes and took 9 hostages. All 9 were killed in a botched rescue attempt. Israel responded with "Operation Wrath of God" — hunting the organization's leaders across Europe.

11 Israeli athletes murdered

Source: Aaron Klein, Striking Back | Simon Reeve, One Day in September
Oct 6, 1973WarsYom Kippur War▼

Egypt and Syria attacked by surprise on Yom Kippur. The IDF suffered heavy blows in the first days — hundreds of tanks destroyed, dozens of planes downed. After massive reserve mobilization, Israel turned the tide, crossed the Suez Canal, and advanced to within 101 km of Cairo.

2,656 Israeli casualties

Source: Abraham Rabinovich, The Yom Kippur War (2004)
Nov 10, 1975AntisemitismResolution 3379 — "Zionism = Racism"▼

The UN General Assembly passed a resolution equating Zionism with racism. Chaim Herzog, Israel's ambassador, tore up the resolution on the podium and declared: "For this falsification of history, and for this hint born in darkness — we respond with the fullest anger." The resolution was revoked in 1991.

Source: UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 | Chaim Herzog speech, 1975
Jul 4, 1976AchievementsOperation Entebbe▼

A hijacked Air France plane with 248 passengers, including 94 Israelis, was flown to Entebbe, Uganda. A Sayeret Matkal commando team flew 4,000 km and rescued the hostages. Yonatan Netanyahu, the force commander, fell — the only officer killed. Considered one of the most brilliant operations in military history.

Yonatan Netanyahu fell

Source: Saul David, Operation Thunderbolt (2015)
Sep 17, 1978PeaceCamp David Accords▼

Prime Minister Menachem Begin and Egyptian President Anwar Sadat signed framework agreements brokered by President Carter. On March 26, 1979, the first peace treaty between Israel and an Arab state was signed. Begin and Sadat received the Nobel Peace Prize.

Source: Camp David Accords (1978) | William Quandt, Camp David
Jun 6, 1982WarsFirst Lebanon War▼

The IDF entered Lebanon to eliminate the PLO, which had been shelling the Galilee. The war extended beyond the original plan. Defense Minister Ariel Sharon led forces to Beirut. The war became controversial in Israel, especially after the Sabra and Shatila massacre.

Source: Ze'ev Schiff & Ehud Ya'ari, Israel's Lebanon War (1984)

Hope for Peace — 1983-1999

1984-1985ZionismOperation Moses — Ethiopian Jews▼

Approximately 8,000 Ethiopian Jews (Beta Israel) were secretly airlifted to Israel via Sudan. Many walked hundreds of kilometers through the desert. About 4,000 died on the journey. A community that waited thousands of years returned to Zion.

~4,000 died on the journey to Sudan

Source: Tudor Parfitt, Operation Moses | USHMM
Dec 9, 1987WarsFirst Intifada▼

A Palestinian uprising erupted across Judea, Samaria, and Gaza. It included mass demonstrations, strikes, stone-throwing, and Molotov cocktails. 160 Israelis and over 1,000 Palestinians were killed. The Intifada ultimately led to the Oslo process.

Source: Benny Morris, Righteous Victims (1999)
1990-1991ZionismGreat Aliyah from the Soviet Union▼

With the collapse of the Soviet Union, approximately one million Jews immigrated to Israel within a decade. Doctors, engineers, scientists, and musicians transformed the country. This aliyah laid the foundations for the "Start-Up Nation" and Israel's high-tech industry.

Source: Israel CBS | Zvi Gitelman, Immigration and Identity
May 24-25, 1991ZionismOperation Solomon — Ethiopian Jews▼

14,325 Ethiopian Jews were airlifted to Israel in just 36 hours — one of the largest airlifts in history. 34 non-stop flights. One C-130 aircraft landed with 1,088 passengers — an unbroken world record. One baby was born in flight.

Source: IDF Archives | Jewish Agency
1991WarsGulf War — Scud Missiles on Israel▼

Iraq launched 39 Scud missiles at Israel during the Gulf War. Israelis sat in sealed rooms with gas masks. Israel did not retaliate militarily — under American pressure — to preserve the coalition against Saddam Hussein.

Source: IDF Archives | Efraim Inbar, Israel's National Security
Sep 13, 1993PeaceOslo Accords▼

Israel and the PLO signed the Oslo Accords on the White House lawn. Rabin and Arafat shook hands, brokered by Clinton. The accords established the Palestinian Authority and opened a peace process that was supposed to end in a final agreement. Rabin, Peres, and Arafat received the Nobel Peace Prize.

Source: Declaration of Principles, Sep 13, 1993 | Yossi Beilin, The Path to Geneva
Oct 26, 1994PeacePeace Treaty with Jordan▼

Israel and Jordan signed a peace treaty in the Arava. King Hussein and Rabin ended 46 years of war. Jordan became the second Arab state to sign peace with Israel, after Egypt.

Source: Israel-Jordan Peace Treaty, October 26, 1994
Nov 4, 1995WarsAssassination of Rabin▼

Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin was assassinated by Yigal Amir at the end of a peace rally at Kings of Israel Square (now Rabin Square), Tel Aviv. The assassination shook Israeli society and changed the course of history. His final words at the rally: "Song of Peace."

Source: Dan Ephron, Killing a King (2015)
1998AchievementsIsrael at 50 — Start-Up Nation Begins▼

Israel celebrated 50 years of independence. The high-tech industry booming: ICQ (1996) sold to AOL for $407 million, Mobileye (1999) was founded, Check Point became a cyber giant. Israel began to be called the "Start-Up Nation."

Source: Dan Senor & Saul Singer, Start-up Nation (2009)

The New Millennium — 2000-2015

2000WarsCamp David II Failure + Second Intifada▼

Prime Minister Barak offered Arafat a Palestinian state on 97% of Judea and Samaria + East Jerusalem. Arafat refused and made no counter-offer. In September 2000, the Second Intifada erupted — a wave of suicide bombings that murdered over 1,000 Israelis in 5 years.

1,137 Israelis murdered in terror attacks

Source: Dennis Ross, The Missing Peace (2004) | Israel MFA
2001-2004AchievementsIsraeli Nobel Prizes▼

A wave of Nobel Prizes for Israelis: Daniel Kahneman (Economics, 2002) for Prospect Theory; Aaron Ciechanover and Avram Hershko (Chemistry, 2004) for discovering the protein degradation mechanism. Israel — with 9 million citizens — the country with the most Nobel laureates per capita.

Source: Nobel Prize Official | Encyclopedia Judaica
2005PeaceGaza Disengagement▼

Prime Minister Ariel Sharon carried out the disengagement plan. 8,000 settlers were evacuated from 21 settlements in the Gaza Strip and 4 in northern Samaria. Soldiers wept, settlers were outraged, families uprooted. Hamas seized Gaza in 2007 and turned it into a rocket launcher.

Source: Israel State Archives | Dov Weisglass, Sharon's advisor
2006WarsSecond Lebanon War▼

Hezbollah kidnapped two reserve soldiers (Eldad Regev and Ehud Goldwasser) and fired rockets at the Galilee. Israel launched a war that lasted 34 days. 4,000 rockets were fired at northern Israel. 121 soldiers and 44 Israeli civilians were killed.

165 Israelis killed

Source: Winograd Commission Report (2008)
2011AchievementsIron Dome — The Military Revolution▼

The Iron Dome system made its first interception on April 7, 2011. An Israeli development by Rafael that intercepts rockets in mid-air — with an interception rate above 90%. The system saved thousands of Israeli and Palestinian lives and changed the rules of the game.

Source: Rafael Advanced Defense Systems | IDF Spokesperson
Mar 19, 2012AntisemitismToulouse Massacre▼

Mohammed Merah, a French-Algerian jihadist terrorist, shot and killed 3 children and a rabbi at the "Ozar HaTorah" Jewish school in Toulouse, France. He had previously murdered 3 French soldiers. The event marked a rise in antisemitic-Islamist violence in Europe.

4 killed at the Jewish school

Source: BBC | Le Monde
2014WarsOperation Protective Edge▼

A major military operation in Gaza following the kidnapping and murder of 3 Israeli teenagers (Gilad Shaer, Naftali Fraenkel, Eyal Yifrach) and a wave of rocket fire. Hamas launched 4,594 rockets. The IDF discovered and destroyed terror tunnels beneath the border.

73 Israeli casualties (67 soldiers, 6 civilians)

Source: IDF Spokesperson | Israel MFA
2013AchievementsSorek Desalination Plant▼

The world's largest desalination plant was inaugurated near Ashkelon. Israel became a water superpower — producing over 80% of its drinking water from desalinated seawater. From a drought-stricken country to a nation exporting water technology to the world.

Source: IDE Technologies | Seth Siegel, Let There Be Water

The Current Era — 2016-2023

Oct 27, 2018AntisemitismTree of Life Synagogue Massacre — Pittsburgh▼

A white supremacist gunman shot and murdered 11 worshippers at the "Tree of Life" synagogue in Pittsburgh, USA — the deadliest antisemitic attack in American history. The victims were aged 54-97. The event stunned the American Jewish community.

11 killed

Source: FBI | New York Times, October 27, 2018
Sep 15, 2020PeaceAbraham Accords▼

Israel signed normalization agreements with the UAE, Bahrain, Morocco, and Sudan. A historic breakthrough — Arab states recognizing Israel without requiring resolution of the Palestinian conflict. Direct flights, embassies, trade, and tourism.

Source: Abraham Accords Declaration, Sep 15, 2020 | White House
2021WarsOperation Guardian of the Walls▼

Hamas launched 4,360 rockets at Israel over 11 days. Iron Dome intercepted 90% of rockets threatening populated areas. Additionally, ethnic riots erupted in mixed cities in Israel — Arab-Jewish violence in Lod, Acre, and Jaffa.

Source: IDF Spokesperson | Israel MFA
Oct 7, 2023AntisemitismOctober 7 Massacre▼

3,000 Hamas terrorists breached the border fence and for hours murdered, raped, tortured, and burned. 1,200 Israelis were murdered — civilians in their homes, the Nova music festival, kibbutzim. 253 were kidnapped to Gaza. The deadliest day for the Jewish people since the Holocaust. The largest massacre in a single day in Israel's history.
