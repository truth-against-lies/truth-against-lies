🎣 Streams & Sectors of the Jewish People

One People — A Hundred Faces
❝Why was Man created alone? To declare the greatness of the Holy One: a person stamps many coins with one die and they all look alike. But the Holy One stamps every person with the die of the First Man — and not one of them looks like another.❞ Talmud Bavli, Sanhedrin 38a
Updated: March 2026

📚 In Brief

  • The Jewish people contain dozens of streams, sectors, and ethnic communities — religious, secular, ethnic, and political
  • From Satmar Hasidim who reject the State to Religious Zionists who see it as “the beginning of redemption”
  • Before the Holocaust, entire streams were erased — the Bund, Salonika Jewry, entire Hasidic courts
  • Despite all internal differences — for our enemies, there is no difference. A Jew is a Jew.

💡 Why Does This Matter?

The Jewish people are not a monolith. Over 3,500 years, diverse communities, traditions, worldviews, and ways of life have developed within it — sometimes similar, sometimes contradictory. Understanding this diversity is essential to understanding Jewish civilization, and to understanding why the notion of “the Jews” as a single homogeneous body is a fundamental error.

This page maps all currently existing streams and sectors, explains the differences between them, reveals what they think of each other, and at the end — reminds us what the Holocaust destroyed, and why despite everything, we are one people.

📜 The Orthodox Spectrum

Orthodox Judaism holds that the Torah — Written and Oral — is the word of God binding on all Jews. But within Orthodoxy itself, there are enormous differences.

★ Chabad-Lubavitch

Hasidic ~90,000 core + millions served

Founded: 1775, by Rabbi Shneur Zalman of Liadi.
Name: Acronym for Chochmah-Binah-Da'at (Wisdom-Understanding-Knowledge). Combines Kabbalah with intellectual contemplation.
Unique: The only Hasidic movement that actively engages in outreach to all Jews worldwide. 4,900 emissary families in 100 countries, 3,500 institutions. Chabad Houses serve as community centers for any Jew — regardless of observance level.
Controversy: The Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson (d. 1994), was considered by many followers to be the Messiah. “Meshichists” still believe he is alive — a position other Haredi groups consider problematic.

★ Satmar

Hasidic ~300,000 — largest court worldwide

Founded: Post-WWII in New York by Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum.
Core belief: Extreme anti-Zionism on theological grounds. Believe establishing a Jewish state before the Messiah is forbidden (based on the “Three Oaths” in Talmud Ketubot). Reject the State of Israel entirely.
Split: After Rebbe Moshe Teitelbaum’s death (2006), split into two factions: Aharon Teitelbaum (Kiryas Joel) and Zalman Teitelbaum (Williamsburg).
Views on others: Consider Zionist Jews (even religious ones) as heretics. Reform/Conservative are not legitimate Judaism. Even criticize other Haredi groups for insufficient anti-Zionism.

★ Breslov

Hasidic Tens of thousands (decentralized)

Founded: By Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (1772-1810), great-grandson of the Baal Shem Tov.
Unique: Emphasizes joy, faith, and simplicity. Core practice: Hitbodedut — an hour daily of personal conversation with God. Known for ecstatic prayer with clapping, singing, and dancing.
No living Rebbe: Rabbi Nachman did not appoint a successor — 200+ years without an Admor. Other Hasidic groups sometimes call them “the Dead Hasidim.”
Na Nach: Sub-group following the mystical formula “Na-Nach-Nachma-Nachman Me’Uman.” Known for white kippot, speaker vans, and street dancing.
Uman: ~50,000 pilgrims travel to Rabbi Nachman’s grave in Uman, Ukraine, every Rosh Hashanah. 8% of all Israeli Jews have visited at least once.

★ Ger, Belz, Vizhnitz, Bobov — Other Major Courts

Hasidic Tens of thousands of families each

Ger: ~11,600 households. Second-largest court. More rationalist approach. Dominant political force in United Torah Judaism. Known for strict modesty regulations.
Belz: ~7,000 households. The Great Synagogue of Belz in Jerusalem is among the world’s largest. Left the Edah HaChareidis in 1979.
Vizhnitz: ~10,500 households. Charismatic-populist tradition centered on the tzaddik.
Bobov: ~100,000 Hasidim pre-war. Only ~300 survived. Rebuilt in Borough Park, Brooklyn.

📚 Lithuanian / Yeshivish (Litvaks)

Haredi-Lithuanian Hundreds of thousands

Approach: Deep intellectual Talmud study — contrasting the emotional/mystical Hasidic approach. Legacy of the Vilna Gaon (1720-1797) who fiercely opposed Hasidism.
Institutions: Lakewood (Beth Medrash Govoha) — 9,000+ students. Mir Yeshiva in Jerusalem — the world’s largest yeshiva. Ponevezh in Bnei Brak.
Kollel system: Married men study Torah full-time with a stipend. Torah study is the supreme value.
Views on others: See themselves as the gold standard of Torah scholarship. Critical of Hasidim for “insufficient Talmud rigor.” Reject Reform/Conservative entirely. Ambivalent toward Religious Zionism.

⚖ Sephardic Haredi (Shas)

Haredi-Sephardic Hundreds of thousands of supporters

Key figure: Rabbi Ovadia Yosef (1920-2013), former Sephardi Chief Rabbi.
Party: Shas, founded 1984 — represents Sephardic and Mizrahi Haredi Jews. Consistently wins 8-11 Knesset seats.
Unique: Follows Sephardic halachic tradition (more lenient in some areas). Combines strict observance with ethnic pride. First Haredi party to join the World Zionist Organization (2010).
Contribution: R’ Ovadia recognized Ethiopian Jews as halachically Jewish (1973). Supported their immigration. Built an entire Sephardic yeshiva network (“El HaMa’ayan”).

💪 Religious Zionism / Dati Leumi

Religious-Zionist ~12% of Israeli Jews

Three pillars: The Land of Israel, the People of Israel, the Torah of Israel. See the State of Israel as religiously significant — many call it “Atchalta De’Geulah” (the beginning of redemption).
Practical: Combine Torah study with secular education, military service, and careers. Men wear knitted kippot. Boys attend Hesder yeshivot, girls do National Service.
Views on others: Critical of Haredim for not serving in the IDF. Respectful but distant from Reform/Conservative. Complex relationship with secular Israelis.

⚔ Hardal (Haredi-Leumi)

Hardal ~1% of Israeli Jews

Hybrid: Haredi-level strictness + Zionist commitment to the Land. Believe Israel is the beginning of redemption. Reject secular liberalism. Full gender separation.
Military: Unlike mainstream Haredim, they serve in the IDF — many in elite combat units. Disproportionate influence on the military and education despite small numbers.

✶ ✶ ✶

🌐 Non-Orthodox Denominations

These movements were founded mainly in the 19th and 20th centuries, from the belief that Judaism must evolve and adapt to the modern world. Orthodoxy does not recognize them as representing authentic Judaism.

🌐 Reform / Progressive Judaism

Reform~2 million — largest US denomination

Founded: Mid-19th century Germany. Founders: Abraham Geiger, Samuel Holdheim.
Beliefs: Judaism has always evolved and must continue adapting. Individual autonomy is paramount. Torah is divinely inspired but not literally divine. Full gender egalitarianism, LGBTQ+ inclusion. Recognizes patrilineal descent (since 1983 — a major break from traditional matrilineal definition).
Services: Primarily in local language. Mixed seating. Women and LGBTQ+ serve as rabbis. Kashrut and Shabbat are personal choices.
In Israel: Tiny presence (~5-8%). No official recognition. Reform rabbis cannot perform legal marriages or conversions.
Trend: Declining — projected 46% decline in ages 30-69 over 50 years.

📜 Conservative / Masorti Judaism

Conservative~1.1-2 million

Founded: Late 19th century as the “Positive-Historical School” of Zacharias Frankel.
Beliefs: Halakha is both binding and subject to historical development. Uses modern research alongside traditional methods. Maintains traditional practices (kashrut, Shabbat, Hebrew in prayer) while allowing change through rabbinic process.
Egalitarian: Women rabbis since 1985. LGBTQ+ rabbis since 2006.
Trend: Declining significantly — was once the largest US denomination, now ~17%.

🔭 Reconstructionism

Reconstructionist~100,000

Founder: Rabbi Mordecai Kaplan (1881-1983).
Beliefs: Judaism is an evolving civilization, not just a religion. Encompasses religion, culture, language, art, history, and social organization. God is understood non-traditionally. Halakha is non-binding but serves as the basis for ongoing practice.
First to: Ordain LGBTQ+ rabbis (1984).

✨ Jewish Renewal

RenewalSmall

Founder: Rabbi Zalman Schachter-Shalomi (1924-2014), a Chabad-trained rabbi who broke away.
Unique: Combines Hasidic ecstatic prayer with contemporary values: gender egalitarianism, environmentalism, progressive politics. Draws on Kabbalah and meditation. One of the few movements bridging Hasidic mysticism with progressive politics.

🧠 Humanistic Judaism

Humanistic~10,000-50,000

Founder: Rabbi Sherwin Wine (1928-2007).
Beliefs: Nontheistic Judaism. Celebrates Jewish culture, history, and holidays without reference to God. Emphasizes human-centered ethics and rational thinking. Judaism = the cultural-historical experience of the Jewish people, not a divinely-commanded religion.
Views ON them: Almost all other Jewish groups consider this an oxymoron — “Judaism without God is not Judaism.”

🌎 Ethnic & Cultural Communities

Beyond the religious-ideological division, the Jewish people are composed of diverse ethnic-cultural communities, each with unique traditions, customs, language, and music.

Ashkenazi Jews

Ethnic~65-70% of world Jewry

Origin: Central and Eastern Europe (Germany, Poland, Russia, Lithuania).
Traditions: Yiddish language, liturgical customs (Nusach Ashkenaz), distinctive foods (gefilte fish, cholent, kugel), halakha per the Rema.
Dominance: Controlled most institutions in Israel and the diaspora. Created the Zionist movement, dominated Israeli politics, academia, judiciary, and military leadership for decades.

Sephardi Jews

EthnicHistorically prestigious community

Origin: Iberian Peninsula (Spain and Portugal) before the 1492 expulsion.
Traditions: Ladino language (Judeo-Spanish), follow the Shulchan Aruch of R’ Yosef Karo directly, distinct liturgy, generally more lenient halachic rulings than Ashkenazim.
After 1492: Dispersed to the Ottoman Empire (Turkey, Greece, Balkans, North Africa), Netherlands, Italy.

Mizrahi Jews

Ethnic~50% of Israeli Jews

Origin: Middle East and North Africa (Iraq, Iran, Yemen, Morocco, Tunisia, Libya, Egypt, Syria).
Tensions with Ashkenazim: One of the deepest fault lines in Israeli society. 1950s: Mizrahi immigrants treated as “backward Orientals,” placed in transit camps. Income gap: Ashkenazi income 36% higher (2004). Ashkenazim twice as likely to attend university. No Mizrahi Prime Minister ever.
Response: “Black Panthers” movement (1971), founding of Shas (1984), increasing integration through intermarriage.

Beta Israel (Ethiopian Jews)

Ethnic~171,600 in Israel

Origin: Amhara and Tigray regions of northern Ethiopia.
Immigration: Operation Moses (1984), Operation Solomon (1991).
Unique: Followed the Torah (“Orit” in Ge’ez) in complete isolation. Did not know the Talmud, Hebrew, or post-biblical traditions. Believed they were the only remaining Jews in the world.
Recognition: R’ Ovadia Yosef ruled them halachically Jewish (1973). Some Ashkenazi authorities required conversion.
Discrimination: 1990s — Israel’s blood bank systematically destroyed blood donated by Ethiopian Jews. Income ~40% below average. Highest poverty rate among Israeli Jews. Massive protests in 2015 and 2019 after police killings.

Yemenite Jews (Teimanim)

Ethnic~400,000+ descendants in Israel

Origin: Yemen — one of the oldest diaspora communities.
Immigration: Operation Magic Carpet (1949-1950), ~49,000 immigrants.
Traditions: Torah reading with Aramaic translation (often by a child), Hebrew pronunciation considered closest to ancient Hebrew, long sidelocks, henna ceremony, unique music and dance traditions.
Trauma: “Yemenite Children Affair” (1948-1954) — allegations that hundreds of Yemenite children were stolen and given to Ashkenazi families.

Indian Jews

Ethnic~5,000 remain in India

Three communities:
Bene Israel: ~67,000 total. Legend says 14 shipwreck survivors landed near Mumbai. Preserved the Shema, Shabbat, circumcision, and some dietary laws in total isolation.
Cochin Jews: ~2,500 in 1948; almost all emigrated to Israel.
Baghdadi Jews: 19th-century immigrants from Iraq, Syria, Iran.
Remarkable fact: India has had virtually zero antisemitism throughout Jewish history there.

Karaite Jews

Ethno-religious~30,000-50,000 in Israel

Origin: 8th-9th century split from Rabbinic Judaism in Mesopotamia.
Beliefs: Reject the Oral Torah (Talmud) entirely. Follow only the Written Torah (Bible). Adhere to the plain meaning of the text.
Key differences: Don’t separate dairy and meat (Talmudic), some don’t celebrate Hanukkah (post-biblical), full prostration in prayer, patrilineal descent, no lights at all on Shabbat.
Status: Recognized as Jews for the Law of Return. The Chief Rabbinate does not accept their marriages or conversions.

Samaritans

Ethno-religious (debated)~900 — world’s smallest ethno-religious group

Beliefs: Consider themselves the true Israelites. Accept only the Five Books of Moses — reject all other books and all rabbinic literature. Mount Gerizim (not Jerusalem) is their holy mountain.
Practices: Maintain a High Priesthood. Slaughter and eat lambs on Passover eve — the most ancient sacrifice still performed.
Location: ~460 in Holon (Israel), ~380 in Kiryat Luza (Mt. Gerizim). Recovered from a low of ~146 in 1917.
Status: Most Jews do not consider Samaritans Jewish. They define themselves as Israelites, not “Jews.”

Romaniote Jews (Greece)

Ethnic~5,500 in Greece — 35 Romaniotes in Ioannina

Origin: Indigenous Jews of Greece. 2,300+ years continuous presence — the longest continuous Jewish presence in Europe.
Traditions: Followed the Jerusalem Talmud (most Jews follow the Babylonian). Unique synagogue layout. Spoke Yevanic (Judeo-Greek) written in Hebrew characters.
Holocaust: Devastating. Most Greek Jews murdered. Survivors in their 80s-90s, customs at risk of extinction.

Italian Jews (Italkim)

Ethnic~28,000 in Italy

Origin: Jews in Italy since the 2nd century BCE — one of the oldest continuous diaspora communities.
Traditions: Italian Rite (Nusach Italiani) — a liturgy distinct from both Ashkenazi and Sephardic. First printed 1486. Scholars believe it closely matches the ancient Land of Israel liturgy.
Holocaust: Mussolini’s racial laws (1938). ~7,500 killed (17%). Unlike Eastern European communities, Italian Jewry survived and still maintains its distinctive traditions.

🏛 Political & Cultural Identities

Secular / Hiloni

Cultural-Political~45% of Israeli Jews — largest category

Jewish identity is primarily ethnic/national, not religious. Most believe in Jewish peoplehood but not necessarily in God. May observe some traditions (Passover seder, Yom Kippur fast) as cultural practices. 73% see the Haredi-secular tension as one of the two most severe conflicts in Israeli society. 80% support requiring Haredim to serve in the IDF.

Masorti / Traditional (Israeli sense)

Cultural~29-33% of Israeli Jews

Not the same as Conservative/Masorti movement: In Israel, “Masorti” means “traditional” — people who observe some laws selectively, believe in God, keep some kashrut and Shabbat, but don’t identify as “religious” or “secular.”
Bridge community: The most diverse group. Most likely to have friends across religious lines. The only potential bridge between Israel’s divided Jewish tribes.
Origin: Predominantly Mizrahi/Sephardic. This was the default Judaism in Middle Eastern communities — tradition without rigid European categorization.

Labor Zionism

Political-Historical

Founders: A.D. Gordon, David Ben-Gurion, Berl Katznelson.
Beliefs: Merged socialism with Jewish nationalism. Jewish state only through the working class — aliyah, kibbutzim, moshavim, “conquest of labor.”
Legacy: Dominated the Zionist movement and Israel from 1930 to 1977. Created the kibbutzim, Histadrut, and virtually all early state institutions. The Ashkenazi intellectual elite it created still influences academia, media, and courts.

Revisionist Zionism

Political-Historical

Founder: Ze’ev Jabotinsky (1925).
Beliefs: Maximalist Jewish state on both sides of the Jordan. Jewish army. Economic liberalism (opposed Labor’s socialism). Emphasis on romantic/national aspects.
Legacy: Menachem Begin (Jabotinsky’s successor) founded Likud. Has dominated Israeli politics since 1977. Netanyahu is the political heir. The dominant ideology in Israel today.

❌ Anti-Zionist Haredim (Satmar + Neturei Karta)

Anti-Zionist

Satmar (see above): ~300,000. Refuse to recognize Israel. Don’t take government money. Don’t vote in elections.
Neturei Karta: ~5,000 followers. Believe Israel’s existence is “the ultimate heresy.” Attended the 2006 Holocaust-denial conference in Tehran. Met with Ahmadinejad. Maintain ties with Iran’s government.
Status: Universally condemned by virtually all Jewish groups, including Satmar itself (which sees engagement with Holocaust deniers as crossing a red line). A minority within a minority.

⚖ What They Think of Each Other

The tensions within the Jewish people are real and deep. Here are the major fault lines:

⚔ Orthodox vs. Non-Orthodox: The Conversion & Western Wall Crisis

The problem: Orthodoxy only recognizes conversions by Orthodox rabbis. Israel’s Chief Rabbinate has sole legal authority over conversion, marriage, and divorce.
Patrilineal descent: Since 1983, Reform recognizes children of Jewish fathers (not just mothers) as Jewish — creating a population that Reform considers Jewish but Orthodoxy does not.
Western Wall crisis (2026): The Knesset voted 56-47 to advance a bill giving the Chief Rabbinate full control over all prayer at the Western Wall. Egalitarian prayer (men and women together) — how 85% of world Jews worship — would be deemed “desecration,” punishable by up to 7 years in prison.

“A direct affront to Jews in Israel, North America, and across the globe” — Reform Movement response

🔥 Secular vs. Haredi: Conscription, Budgets, and Service

73% of Israelis see the Haredi-secular tension as one of the two most severe conflicts.
Conscription: The most explosive issue. Haredi men have been exempt from military service since 1948. In October 2025, 200,000 Haredim blocked roads in Jerusalem protesting conscription enforcement. A 15-year-old boy died during the demonstration.
Funding: Haredim receive government stipends for full-time Torah study. Secular Israelis resent funding a growing population that neither works nor serves.
Polarization: Israel isn’t uniformly moving toward more or less religion — the observant are becoming more observant and the secular more secular.

Ashkenazi vs. Mizrahi/Sephardi

One of the most painful fault lines in Israeli society:
1950s: Mizrahi immigrants placed in transit camps and development towns.
Economic gap: Ashkenazi income 36% higher. Twice as likely to attend university.
Representation: No Mizrahi Prime Minister ever. No Mizrahi head of the Supreme Court. Academia overwhelmingly Ashkenazi.
Shas party: Founded in 1984 specifically to fight Ashkenazi discrimination and represent Sephardic/Mizrahi interests.
Today: Intermarriage increasing. Social integration improving but structural inequalities persist.

👁 What Haredim Think of Everyone

See themselves as the sole authentic custodians of halakha:
On Religious Zionism: “Compromisers” — diluting Torah values for modernity.
On Reform: Not Judaism. A form of assimilation. “Destruction of Torah from within.” Their rabbis aren’t rabbis. Their conversions are invalid.
On Conservative: Slightly better than Reform but still illegitimate. A “slippery slope.”
On Secular Jews: Lost Jews who need to be brought back (kiruv/outreach). Not enemies, but misguided.

🌐 What Reform/Conservative Think of Orthodoxy

Rigidity: Orthodoxy is seen as rigid, exclusionary, and patriarchal.
Monopoly: The Orthodox monopoly in Israel = violation of religious freedom.
Rejecting the majority: 85% of Diaspora Jews worship in egalitarian settings. The Orthodox refusal to accept this is seen as rejecting the majority of world Jewry.
The Western Wall bill: Called a “wake-up call” — proof that the Israeli government under Haredi influence would criminalize how most world Jews pray.

🔎 “Who Is a Jew?” — The Growing Crisis

The problem: After several generations, many people are considered Jewish by some denominations but not others. A Reform convert cannot marry in Israel. A child of a Jewish father and non-Jewish mother is Jewish according to Reform but not according to Orthodox, Conservative, or traditional halakha.
The future: Orthodox/Haredi communities are the only ones growing. Reform and Conservative are shrinking. Within 50 years, the majority of actively-affiliated Jews worldwide may be Orthodox — but millions considered Jewish by some will not be recognized by others.

🕯 Before the Holocaust — What Was Destroyed

The Holocaust didn’t just murder six million Jews. It destroyed entire streams, cultures, languages, and traditions that can never return. ~16.6 million Jews before the Holocaust, ~15.8 million today — 80+ years and we still haven’t recovered demographically.

🕯 Destroyed in the Holocaust

The Bund — Jewish Socialist Movement

Founded: 1897 in Vilna (same year as the First Zionist Congress).
Beliefs: Jewish socialism + cultural autonomy within their countries of residence (opposed Zionism). Promoted Yiddish as the Jewish national language. Fought for political and cultural rights in Eastern Europe without a separate state.
Size: One of the largest Jewish political movements pre-war, especially in Poland, Russia, and Lithuania.
What was destroyed: The Holocaust eliminated its membership base. The creation of Israel and the shift from Yiddish to Hebrew/English made its vision irrelevant.

🕯 Destroyed in the Holocaust

Entire Hasidic Courts Wiped Out

Pre-war Poland was the world center of Hasidic Judaism. Courts destroyed:
Alexander: One of Poland’s three largest courts. Annihilated.
Bobov: ~100,000 Hasidim pre-war. Only ~300 survived.
Piaseczno: Rabbi Kalonymus Kalman Shapira wrote his “Aish Kodesh” sermons inside the Warsaw Ghetto before being murdered at Treblinka.
Dozens of smaller courts completely erased with no survivor to rebuild.

🕯 Destroyed in the Holocaust

The Lithuanian Yeshiva World

The great yeshivot that defined Jewish intellectual life:
Volozhin (1803) — “the Mother of All Yeshivot.”
Slobodka (Kovno) — “the Mother of Yeshivot.” Emphasized Mussar (ethical self-improvement). Closed 1941 when Nazis occupied Lithuania; most students murdered.
Mir — The only yeshiva that escaped intact to Shanghai. Rebuilt in Jerusalem (now the world’s largest yeshiva).
Ponevezh, Radun, Telshe, Kelm — all destroyed, some rebuilt in Israel or the US.

🕯 95% Killed

Salonika Jewry — “Jerusalem of the Balkans”

Pre-war: 55,200 Jews — two-thirds of the city’s population. The world’s largest Ladino-speaking community. The port closed on Saturdays. Known as “the Mother of Israel.”
Destruction: 95% killed. 43,850 deported, only ~1,950 returned alive (~4% survival rate).
Language death: The last generation to speak Ladino at home was the Holocaust generation. Today only a handful of Jews in Thessaloniki speak Ladino.
What was lost: An entire civilization — 500 years of continuous Sephardic culture, unique music, literature, journalism (multiple Ladino newspapers).

🕯 Destroyed in the Holocaust

German Reform Judaism — The Origin Community

The birthplace of Reform Judaism in the 1840s. The most integrated Jewish community in Europe. Produced Einstein, Freud, Kafka, Mendelssohn, Heine.
The Holocaust destroyed: Not just the people but the unique synthesis of German Bildung and Jewish identity. ~225,000 Jews in Germany today (mostly from the former Soviet Union), but the original German-Jewish culture is gone forever.

🕯 Destroyed in the Holocaust

The Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment)

Period: Late 18th to late 19th century. Key figure: Moses Mendelssohn.
Beliefs: Integration of Jews into European society through secular education, adoption of local languages and customs, while maintaining Jewish identity. Promoted Hebrew as a literary language.
Legacy: Gave birth to Reform Judaism (taking modernization furthest) and indirectly to Orthodox Judaism (which defined itself in opposition). Also inspired secular Jewish nationalism (Zionism) and Yiddish cultural movements. Its Eastern European variant was physically destroyed in the Holocaust.
