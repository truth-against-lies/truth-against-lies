Antisemitism by Nations

Analysis of different expressions of antisemitism across societies and geographic regions

Last updated: March 2026

In Brief
  • 7 societies and nations that developed unique expressions of antisemitism — a shared pattern in different versions
  • The Arab world: 856,000 Jews expelled (1945–1972), fewer than 4,500 remain. The Hamas Charter calls for killing Jews
  • Poland: the Kielce pogrom (1946) — 42 Holocaust survivors murdered one year after the liberation of Auschwitz
  • Nazi Germany: 400+ anti-Jewish laws, Kristallnacht, and 6 million victims
  • Including: Britain, France, Latin America, and more — each with documentation and sources

The Muslim and Arab World

From medieval religious antisemitism to the modern conflict

The Historical Source: In early Judaism and Islam there were periods of coexistence. However, the Quran contains negative verses about Jews (Surah 5:60 — "those whom Allah cursed and turned into apes and pigs"), and Muhammad expelled and massacred Jewish tribes in Medina (Banu Qurayza, 627 CE — 600-900 Jewish men were beheaded). Under Islamic rule, Jews were "dhimmis" — protected but inferior citizens, who paid the jizya tax and wore identifying markers.

Documented Fact: Between 1948–1972, approximately 850,000 Jews were expelled or forced to flee from Arab and Muslim countries. Their property was confiscated. Jewish communities thousands of years old in Iraq, Egypt, Libya, Yemen, Syria, and Morocco were almost completely eliminated. The Farhud in Iraq (1941): 180 Jews were murdered, 1,000 injured, 900 homes destroyed — inspired by Nazi propaganda spread by the Mufti Haj Amin al-Husseini.

The Invention of Palestinian National Identity: The term "Palestine" is a Roman geographic name (from 135 CE, when the Romans renamed Judea to erase Jewish identity). The Arabs of the region identified for centuries as Syrians, Arabs, or by tribe and family. Palestinian national identity crystallized mainly in the mid-20th century. Zuheir Mohsen, leader of as-Sa'iqa (a Palestinian organization), said in 1977 in an interview with the Dutch newspaper Trouw: "The Palestinian people does not exist... There are no differences between Jordanians, Palestinians, Syrians, and Lebanese. We are all part of one Arab nation."

Suicide Terrorism: During the Second Intifada (2000–2005), more than 140 suicide bombings were carried out against Israeli civilians on buses, in restaurants, shopping centers, and nightclubs. 1,137 Israelis were killed. Hamas, Islamic Jihad, and Fatah sent young people, including women and teenagers, to carry out suicide bombings.

The Hamas Charter (1988): Article 7 calls for the killing of Jews (not Israelis — Jews). Article 22 claims Jews are behind the French Revolution, World War I, Communism, and World War II. Article 32 cites the "Protocols of the Elders of Zion" — a Russian antisemitic forgery from 1903 — as a genuine document. Notably, the content targets Jews as a collective, not merely Israeli policy.

The "Genocide" Claim: The IDF employs early warning systems for civilian populations before strikes ("roof knocking," SMS notifications, leaflets), and an independent judicial system that investigates violations. Demographically, Gaza's population grew from approximately 240,000 in 1948 to over 2 million in 2023 — a figure inconsistent with the legal definition of genocide (for comparison: Rwanda — 800,000 dead in 100 days).

Modern Propaganda: The "Protocols of the Elders of Zion" — a Russian forgery from 1903 — is a bestseller in the Arab world. The Egyptian series "Horseman Without a Horse" (2002, 41 episodes, broadcast on state television) is based on the Protocols and depicts Jews as conspirators. UNRWA (the UN agency for refugees) textbooks contained antisemitic content — including maps without Israel and poems praising suicide bombings.

Sources: Benny Morris, '1948' (2008); Hamas Charter (1988); MEMRI translations; IMPACT-se school textbook reports; Andrew Bostom, 'The Legacy of Islamic Antisemitism' (2008)

Poland and Eastern Europe

Talmudic myths, "Jewish Communism," and hatred that survived the Holocaust

The Talmud Myth: One of the widespread beliefs in Poland (and Eastern Europe) is that the Talmud is filled with hatred toward non-Jews. Antisemitic propaganda (mainly from the medieval Catholic Church and publications like "Talmud Unmasked" by Justinas Pranaitis, 1892) extracted quotes out of context, deliberately mistranslated them, or simply fabricated them. The Talmud consists of 2,711 double-sided pages (5,422 pages) of legal discussion, and no qualified Talmud scholar supports the antisemitic reading.

The Kielce Pogrom (1946): One year after the liberation of Auschwitz, 42 Jewish Holocaust survivors were murdered in a pogrom in the city of Kielce, Poland. The pretext: a blood libel (!) — a rumor that a Christian child had been kidnapped by Jews. In 1946. After the Holocaust. Survivors who lived through Auschwitz were murdered by their Polish neighbors based on the same medieval lie. This event triggered a wave of flight (Bericha) of Holocaust survivors from Poland.

The Żydokomuna Myth ("Jewish Communism"): The belief that Communism is a "Jewish invention" and that Jews ruled Communist Poland. In reality: the Polish United Workers' Party (PZPR) was overwhelmingly Polish. A few individual Jews held high positions — just as Jews were present in every field — but they constituted a tiny percentage. Many Poles were enthusiastic Communists with no connection to Jews. In 1968, the Polish Communist regime itself conducted an antisemitic campaign ("anti-Zionists") and expelled approximately 13,000 Jews from Poland.

Jedwabne (1941): On July 10, 1941, Polish residents of the town of Jedwabne murdered at least 340 (possibly more than 1,000) of the town's Jews — burning them alive in a barn. Not Nazis. Polish neighbors. Historian Jan Tomasz Gross documented this in his book "Neighbors" (2001), causing a national shock in Poland. In 2018, Poland enacted a law forbidding the attribution of Holocaust crimes to the Polish nation — an attempt to silence history.

Russia: The "Protocols of the Elders of Zion" were created by the Russian secret police (Okhrana) around 1903, based on plagiarism from a French book (Maurice Joly, 1864) that had nothing to do with Jews at all. The Pale of Settlement (1791–1917) confined Jews to restricted residential areas. Between 1881–1921, waves of pogroms killed tens of thousands of Jews. Many of the early pogroms were encouraged or planned by the government itself. The "Doctors' Plot" (1953) — Stalin accused Jewish doctors of conspiring to murder Soviet leaders. Only Stalin's death prevented the mass deportation of Jews to Siberia.
Sources: Jan T. Gross, 'Neighbors' (2001); Joanna Tokarska-Bakir, 'Pogrom Cries' (2018); Norman Cohn, 'Warrant for Genocide' (1967)

Nazi Germany

From ideological antisemitism to industrial extermination

The Ideology: The Nazis did not invent antisemitism — they industrialized it. They took 2,000 years of Christian antisemitism, combined it with 19th-century pseudo-scientific racism (racial anthropology, eugenics, social Darwinism), and created an entire system of laws, bureaucracy, and industry whose sole purpose was the extermination of an entire people.

The Ladder of Destruction:
1933: Boycott of Jewish businesses, dismissal from government positions
1935: Nuremberg Laws — revoked citizenship, banned mixed marriages
1938: Kristallnacht — 1,400 synagogues burned, 30,000 Jews sent to camps
1939–1941: Ghettos, organized starvation, mass shootings (Einsatzgruppen killed 1.5 million)
1942: Wannsee Conference — the "Final Solution" became official
1942–1945: Industrial gas chambers at Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Chelmno, Majdanek

The Propaganda Machine: Joseph Goebbels built a propaganda system without precedent. The film "The Eternal Jew" (1940) depicted Jews as rats. The newspaper "Der Stürmer" (Julius Streicher) published weekly antisemitic caricatures. Children were taught antisemitism in schools from age 6. Children's books like "The Poisonous Mushroom" (1938) taught children to identify and hate Jews. The method: repeat the lie enough times and people will start to believe it.

The Structural Contradiction: The Nazis simultaneously claimed that Jews were an inferior race (Untermenschen) and that they controlled the entire world. This contradiction illustrates a structural feature of antisemitism: it does not require internal consistency, but serves as a framework for justifying hatred.

The Numbers: 6 million Jews were murdered — one third of the entire Jewish people. 1.5 million of them were children. Jewish communities a thousand years old in Europe were completely eliminated. The Holocaust is unique in that a modern state dedicated industrial resources — trains, factories, bureaucracy — to the goal of systematic extermination of a people.
Sources: Raul Hilberg, 'The Destruction of the European Jews' (1961/2003); USHMM archives; Yad Vashem Central Database of Shoah Victims' Names

Modern Western Europe

Modern expressions of antisemitism in Western democracies

The New "Anti-Zionism": Activist Natan Sharansky developed the "3D Test" to identify when criticism of Israel crosses into antisemitism: Demonization (Israel = Nazis), Delegitimization (Israel has no right to exist), Double standards (demands of Israel that are made of no other country). Legitimate criticism of Israeli policy is accepted — as with any country. But denying only Israel's right to exist, out of all the world's nations, is antisemitism.

France: The largest Jewish exodus from Europe in the 21st century is happening in France. 2006: Ilan Halimi, a young Jew, was kidnapped, tortured for 24 days, and murdered by the "Gang of Barbarians" — because he was Jewish "and therefore rich." 2012: Mohamed Merah shot and killed 3 children and Rabbi Jonathan Sandler at a Jewish school in Toulouse. 2015: Attack on the Hyper Cacher kosher supermarket in Paris, 4 murdered. Jews are 1% of France's population but 40% of hate crime victims. Approximately 38,000 Jews left France for Israel between 2010–2020.

Britain — The Labour Scandal: Under Jeremy Corbyn (2015–2020), the British Labour Party was investigated by the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) and found responsible for serious breaches of discrimination law — institutional antisemitism. Corbyn called Hamas and Hezbollah "friends," laid a wreath at the grave of an organizer of the Munich Olympic massacre of 1972, and endorsed an artist who painted an antisemitic mural. Thousands of Jews left the party.

University Campuses: The BDS (Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions) movement has become a significant presence on campuses. Jewish students report harassment and exclusion from social justice organizations. Notably, the movement targets exclusively Israel — using human rights language to deny the right of self-determination of one people only.

Sources: EHRC Report on Labour (2020); Natan Sharansky, '3D Test' (2004); ADL Campus Antisemitism reports; CRIF (Conseil Représentatif des Institutions Juives de France)

United States

Antisemitism from the far right, the African-American community, and celebrity culture

The Far Right: In Charlottesville, Virginia (August 2017), neo-Nazis marched with torches chanting "Jews will not replace us." On October 27, 2018, Robert Bowers entered the Tree of Life synagogue in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, and shot 11 worshippers to death. This is the deadliest attack against Jews in American history. He shouted "All Jews must die" before opening fire.

Statistics: In 2022, the ADL documented 3,697 antisemitic incidents in the U.S. — the highest figure since tracking began in 1979. A 36% increase in a single year. 2023 (after October 7): The figure jumped to 8,873 incidents — a 140% increase. Jews are 2.4% of the population but account for over 60% of religious hate crime victims according to the FBI.

Louis Farrakhan and the "Nation of Islam": Farrakhan has called Jews "termites" and "Satans in human clothing." His organization publishes books such as "The Secret Relationship Between Blacks and Jews" accusing Jews of the slave trade — a claim rejected by leading historians (including African-American historians such as Henry Louis Gates Jr.). Notably, this antisemitism uses the language of social justice.

Kanye West (Ye) and Celebrity Culture: In December 2022, West declared "I love Nazis" and praised Hitler on live broadcast. Earlier he had tweeted "Death con 3 on Jewish people." According to ADL reports, there was a sharp increase in antisemitic propaganda from far-right groups citing West. This phenomenon illustrates the impact of normalizing antisemitism by public figures with millions of followers.

Sources: ADL Audit of Antisemitic Incidents (2022, 2023); FBI Hate Crime Statistics; Henry Louis Gates Jr., 'Black Antisemitism' in Transition (1992); Pittsburgh Post-Gazette reporting

Iran and the Islamic Regime

Antisemitism as state policy: Holocaust denial, funding armed organizations, and anti-Israel rhetoric

Institutionalized Holocaust Denial: Former President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad held an international conference on Holocaust denial in Tehran (2006), invited known Holocaust deniers from around the world (including David Duke from the KKK), and declared the Holocaust a "myth." Supreme Leader Khamenei continued this line, claiming there are "serious doubts" about the Holocaust. Scholars note that state-level Holocaust denial may serve as groundwork for future violence.

Funding Armed Organizations: Iran funds and arms Hezbollah (Lebanon), Hamas (Gaza), Islamic Jihad (Gaza), and the Houthis (Yemen). Hezbollah is responsible for the AMIA bombing in Buenos Aires (1994, 85 killed) and the attack on the Israeli Embassy (1992, 29 killed). The stated goal of the Iranian regime: "wiping Israel off the map" — rhetoric that goes beyond political criticism and targets the existence of the state itself.

The Internal Contradiction: Iran claims Israel is racist, while the Iranian regime itself pursues systematic suppression of minorities: Baha'is, Kurds, Arabs, Sunnis, and LGBTQ individuals. According to human rights reports, thousands of executions are carried out annually. The Jewish community in Iran (approximately 8,000 people, down from over 100,000 before the revolution) lives under restrictions and surveillance.

Sources: AMIA investigation records; IAEA reports; US State Department Country Reports on Human Rights; MEMRI translations of Iranian state media

Japan, South Korea, and East Asia

A surprising phenomenon: antisemitism in countries with almost no Jews

The Phenomenon: Approximately 1,000 Jews live in Japan (out of 125 million). In South Korea — a few hundred. And yet, the "Protocols of the Elders of Zion" has been translated and sold in Japan in dozens of editions. Books like "Jewish Control of the World" are bestsellers. The phenomenon of "antisemitism without Jews" demonstrates that antisemitism does not require actual contact with Jews — it is based on images and myths.

The Asian Version: Unlike European antisemitism (hatred), the Asian version is sometimes "positive" in a distorted way: "Jews are smart, control money, we can learn from them." This is still antisemitism — because it is based on stereotypes, not on encounters with real Jews. It transforms Jews from human beings into mythical figures.

Sources: ADL Global 100 Survey; David Goodman & Masanori Miyazawa, 'Jews in the Japanese Mind' (1995)
