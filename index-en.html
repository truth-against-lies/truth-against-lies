Cognitive Biases — Scientific Background

Psychological research shows that every person is subject to cognitive biases that affect information processing. Below are 5 documented phenomena worth knowing when consuming information from any source — including this site.

Illusory Truth Effect

In 1977, researchers Hasher, Goldstein, and Toppino scientifically proved: when we hear a claim again and again — our brain starts to accept it as true. Even if it is a complete lie. In 2015, Fazio showed that even when people know something is untrue — repeating the claim makes them feel it is correct.

This is exactly what happens with slogans like "From the river to the sea" — they are repeated so many times that people stop checking whether they are true.

Cognitive Dissonance

In 1957, psychologist Leon Festinger discovered that when new information contradicts what we already believe — our brain does not accept it gladly. It feels real pain. And because of that pain, most people prefer to reject the new information — rather than update their belief.

This is not stupidity. It is a psychological defense. But if we are aware of it — we can choose differently.

Heuristics and Judgment Biases (Kahneman & Tversky)

Nobel laureate researchers Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky proved (1974) that the human brain uses "shortcuts" (cognitive biases) to decide quickly. The framework of "System 1" (fast, automatic, emotional) and "System 2" (slow, deliberate, rational) was developed in Kahneman's book Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011).

Most of our judgments — including about peoples, nations, and conflicts — are made in System 1. Without checking. Without thinking. We simply "felt" something was right.

This site aims to present information in a way that invites System 2 processing.

Confirmation Bias & Groupthink

Psychologist Peter Wason (1960) showed that humans naturally seek only evidence that supports their opinion — and ignore contradicting evidence. Irving Janis (1972) added: in a group, the effect intensifies severalfold. We want to belong, not to be different. So we adopt the group's opinion — even when it is wrong.

Social networks amplify this: the algorithm shows you only what you already believe. Here we will expose you to what wasn't shown to you.

Awareness of Biases Reduces Their Impact (Debiasing)

Research by Morewedge and colleagues (2015) proved: simply knowing about these biases already begins to free us from them. Participants who learned about cognitive biases improved their decision-making by 30%. And the effect lasted for months.

Awareness of cognitive biases is a first step in critically reading any information, including the content presented on this site.

The purpose of this site is to present documented facts and allow readers to form an independent opinion based on verified information.
